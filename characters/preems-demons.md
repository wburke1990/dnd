# Preem's Demons — the bound cast

Preem's "army of demons" — the servants who staff his tower, run his errands,
pay the party for names, and (eventually) hunt them. Distilled from the
6/8–6/17/26 Discord brainstorm. Companion to [Preem](preem.md) and
[the Second Foundation](../lore/the-second-foundation.md).

> Legend: **[settled]** = agreed in chat · **[loose]** = floated, not locked.
>
> All stat blocks are standard *Monster Manual* demons (linked to 5e.tools).
> Reproduce them from your own books at the table — only the **homebrew**
> rules (demonic restoration; Mul's acid kiss) are written out here.

## The premise

**Every "person" in Preem's tower is a demon in disguise** — a shapechanger or
shapeshifted demon enslaved to him. **[settled]** He summons them with blood
and ancient names; they fetch jewels and spell components so he can summon
more. The party meets them as servants, associates, and helpful strangers long
before they understand what they are dealing with.

## True Names & Planar Binding *(homebrew house rule)*

The mechanical heart of Preem's power, and the campaign's central moral object.
**[settled — "Do it."]**

- Casting **Planar Binding** on a fiend or celestial **while speaking its true
  name auto-succeeds — no saving throw.** Without the true name, Planar Binding
  works as written (the target gets its save, with disadvantage if you summoned
  it, etc.).
- In standard 5e a true name is flavor with no teeth. Here it has teeth. A bound
  demon with *disadvantage* on the save still escapes roughly a quarter of the
  time — over decades, that's a statistical certainty of at least one escape.
  Auto-success is what makes **lifelong, generational bondage** possible, and
  what makes the trap feel airtight.
- This is why **the names are the whole game.** Every name the party pulls from
  the tombs and sells to Mul is another true name in Preem's ledger — another
  demon he can bind beyond any hope of refusal. He has an *army* of true names.
  (See [the names quest](preem.md#the-names-quest--why-he-sends-the-party-to-the-tombs).)
- It is also the engine of the [dream quest's](../encounters/the-dream-quest.md)
  horror: the merchant's demon wife **could not have escaped, ever**, because he
  held her name. Planar binding is meant to feel **horrific** — a true name
  turns a person into a possession, with no roll left to make.
- The endgame turns on it: the party will eventually be handed the
  **volcano-demon's true name** and asked to use it. The dream quest is their
  rehearsal for that choice. (See
  [Preem](preem.md#the-climax--the-volcano-demon-choice).)

## Demonic Restoration *(homebrew house rule)*

The mechanic that makes Preem's servants a **recurring cast** rather than
one-and-done encounters. **[settled — sankaku's design.]**

- A demon with Demonic Restoration that **dies outside the Abyss** is not
  destroyed. It **immediately reforms somewhere in the Abyss at full hit
  points.**
- On death its body **melts into a black liquid that seeps into the ground** —
  think Sauron dissolving in *The Rings of Power*. The party watches the kill
  "stick," then watches it drain away into nothing.
- The demon **comes back in a few sessions**, restored and **PISSED** — but
  bound by Preem's orders. So it works two ways at once: it pursues Preem's
  commands, *and* it **subtly subverts him by helping the party to the extent
  its orders allow.**
- These demons are **quasi-immortal — older than the oldest elves.** They
  **remember things that now survive only in ancient tombs and decaying
  scrolls.** That makes a recurring demon a living source of exactly the lore
  the tomb-names quest is digging up. **[settled]**

> Table effect: killing one of Preem's demons is never clean. It buys time, it
> makes an enemy, and it occasionally buys an unreliable ally with a very long
> memory.

## Mul — the Yochlol associate

The face of Preem's operation; the voice on the other end of the sending
stone. **[settled]**

- **What it is:** a **Yochlol**, shapeshifted to look like an **elf**.
  *Demon, CR 10 — [5e.tools](https://5e.tools/bestiary.html#yochlol_xmm).*
- **Name:** **Mul** — the Asimov *"the Mule"* easter egg. *"Pretty safe to say
  this group is not a sci-fi group, so I doubt they'll figure out Mul is The
  Mule. Not going to overthink that one."*
- **Role:** holds the **paired sending stone**. When the party relays names, it
  replies **in Elvish**: *"Thank you for the names. I am dispatching your
  payment now."* On later visits to the tower, **Mul is the one who greets
  them.**
- **Voice & manner:** a **very nasally voice**; plays **unassuming** and
  harmless on purpose; **sometimes refers to itself as "we."** **[settled]**
- **Restoration:** has Demonic Restoration (above). When the party kills it,
  it comes back wearing a new face. Worked example from the chat: kill Mul one
  week, and *"Mul's back, shapeshifted into that sweet girl you met at the
  tavern — and now her kisses do 6d6 + 4 acid damage."*

### Mul's Acid Kiss *(homebrew, for the tavern-girl form or any seduction gambit)*

> **Acid Kiss.** *Melee Spell Attack.* On a hit, the target takes
> **6d6 + 4 acid damage.** Use when Mul has lured a PC into intimate range in a
> disguised form — the payoff for trusting a sweet stranger who is, in fact,
> the demon they "killed" last session.

## The Quasit — the paymaster

- **What it is:** an **invisible quasit**.
  *Demon, CR 1 — [5e.tools](https://5e.tools/bestiary.html#quasit_xmm).*
- **Role:** while the party is **long resting**, it drops off **5 gp per name**
  they relayed — invisibly, leaving the coin to be found on waking. **[settled]**
- Keeps the names-for-coin economy feeling like clockwork magic rather than a
  transaction with a monster.

## The familiar — the "psychic bat"

A **second quasit**, separate from the paymaster: the one Preem gifted to
**Jasper** in the campaign's opening scene, disguised as a psychic-bat
**familiar** and planted as a **spy.** It speaks only in Jasper's mind at first,
and the player isn't in on it either. Full treatment in
[The Psychic Bat](the-psychic-bat.md). **[settled]**

## Table notes

- Lean on **Demonic Restoration** to build relationships, not just fights.
  A demon the party "beat" and then meets again — changed, resentful, quietly
  useful — is more memorable than a fresh stat block.
- The disguised servants in the tower are a **slow-burn reveal**. Let the party
  notice the staff are *off* (never eating, never aging, a little too attentive)
  long before they prove it.
- Demons' **ancient memory** is a pressure valve: when the tomb-names plot needs
  a lore drop, a bound demon who remembers the old empire firsthand can deliver
  it — at a price, and never quite straight.
