# Pax Verdant
## Aasimar Monk — played by Andrew

---

## Basic Information

**Race:** Aasimar (blue-green skin, faint gold-green lines beneath the surface)
**Class:** Monk (Warrior of the Elements path likely)
**Player:** Andrew
**Affiliation:** [PLACEHOLDER — ecoterrorist organization name], alongside Sarric and Jasper

Pax is a blue-green Aasimar monk who believes he has been chosen by the living world itself. Trained in a remote wilderness monastery, exiled after a series of escalating incidents, he now wanders as a guide, healer, survivalist, and extremely unstable prophet of ecological correction.

The key ambiguity is simple: **Pax may be delusional. Pax may be chosen. Pax may be both.**

---

## Appearance

Pax is tall, athletic, and unsettlingly striking. His skin is blue-green, like river stone beneath moonlight, and faint gold-green lines run beneath the surface like roots under soil. His eyes are pale sea-glass and seem too bright in darkness. His hair is short-to-medium, messy, and wild, usually looking as though he just woke from a prophetic fever dream in the rain.

He dresses like a monk, guide, and forest hermit stitched into one person: travel wraps, worn cloth, weathered sashes, seed charms, bits of moss, cartographic scraps, small vials, and talismans of bark, bone, stone, and glass.

He carries a moss-covered quarterstaff called **The Compost Staff**. Pax insists the staff is not a weapon. He calls it *"a branch with a mandate."*

## Backstory

### The Verdant Fist Monastery

Pax was raised by an isolated wilderness monastery known as **The Verdant Fist** — located deep in an old forest, high in a moss-covered mountain range, on the edge of a sacred marsh, or wherever best fits the campaign world.

The Verdant Fist teaches that the body is a bridge between spirit and soil. Its monks are not druids, but they live close to druidic traditions. They study breath, movement, survival, restraint, terrain, weather, and the unseen rhythm of living things. Their philosophy is built on balance: civilization and wilderness are both part of the world, but both must remain in proper proportion. The monks serve as guides, mediators, scouts, messengers, and protectors of remote places. They are not anti-civilization extremists — at least, not officially.

Pax was a promising student: quick, perceptive, disciplined when watched, and unusually sensitive to the natural world. He learned the forms quickly — moving over wet stone without slipping, disappearing into undergrowth, tracking animals through poor terrain, surviving in the wild with almost nothing. But he also showed a tendency toward what the monastery politely called **interpretive excess**.

> Where other students heard the wind, Pax heard instructions.
> Where others saw animal behavior, Pax saw testimony.
> Where others meditated on balance, Pax began asking who would enforce it.

### The Green Revelation

Pax's break from the monastery began during a ritual fast in a moss-covered cave beneath the monastery grounds. He claims he experienced what he calls **The Green Revelation**.

According to Pax, the roots spoke first. Then the beetles. Then the mud. Then the river under the mountain. Finally, something vast and wounded opened its awareness to him: not a god exactly, not a spirit exactly, but the living world itself. He says he saw forests cut open, rivers poisoned, mountains hollowed, animals driven mad by smoke and steel, and cities spreading like mold with paperwork. He says he saw the world try to heal itself and fail because no one was willing to become its hand.

When Pax emerged, he was changed. His skin carried a faint green-gold glow that had not been there before. His eyes looked brighter. Plants seemed to lean toward him, though this may have been coincidence. He began to speak of *"the world's legal standing,"* *"the testimony of moss,"* and *"the corrective mandate of soil."*

The monastery's elders believed the vision was the result of fasting, exhaustion, latent Aasimar magic, and Pax's already unstable imagination. Pax believed the elders had failed their final test.

### The Incidents

Pax's exile did not happen all at once. It followed a string of incidents that escalated from eccentric to dangerous:

- **The Livestock Liberation** — Pax released the monastery's goats and pack animals after declaring them "laborers under a coercive agrarian regime." Several returned on their own. Pax considered this "a negotiated settlement."
- **The Swamp Tea Affair** — Pax replaced the abbot's tea with swamp water because he believed the abbot needed "mineral truth." The abbot was ill for two days. Pax maintained that the abbot had "received clarity."
- **The Cart Dismantling** — A visiting merchant woke to find his cart taken apart and arranged in a spiral. Pax claimed the wheels "smelled imperial" and that the wood wanted "a less circular life."
- **The Garden Vigil** — Pax was found standing barefoot in the vegetable garden before dawn, apologizing to radishes he had not personally harmed.
- **The Fence Sermon** — The final incident involved a visiting noble, three ceremonial trees, a compost pit, and a speech Pax titled *"On the Moral Cowardice of Fences."*

After this, the elders exiled Pax from the Verdant Fist. Pax does not describe it as exile. He says he was **"composted into purpose."**

### Life After Exile

Since leaving the monastery, Pax has wandered as a guide, healer, scout, and wilderness problem-solver — and he is genuinely capable. He can navigate hostile terrain, read signs of weather and wildlife, identify edible plants, guide travelers through remote areas, and keep people alive in bad conditions.

He is also a problem for anyone exploiting the natural world too aggressively. He has been accused of sabotage, trespass, unlawful release of animals, destruction of industrial equipment, disruption of trade, impersonating a religious authority, inciting shrubbery-related panic, and making threats on behalf of rivers. He denies some of these charges. Not all.

He later fell in with Sarric and Jasper, becoming the third member of their organization.

## Personality and Beliefs

- His worldview is not coherent enough to be a formal ideology. He does not hate all civilization — he can enter towns, speak with officials, take payment, work with merchants, and cooperate with organized groups. But he believes civilization is always **on probation**.
- His general position: *Civilization may continue, but only after it apologizes.*
- Speaks in the language of ecology, law, and testimony all at once — moss has standing, soil has a mandate, rivers can issue threats through him.
- Sincere, capable, and warm one moment; alarmingly literal in his convictions the next. The instability is real and is part of the character.
- Believes he has been chosen. Whether he is right is the open question of his arc.

## The Mystery of Pax's Power

Pax is an Aasimar, so some of his supernatural nature may be celestial in origin. But Pax interprets every supernatural sign through the language of the living world. He does not describe his light as holy — he calls it **photosynthetic judgment**. He does not think his healing comes from the heavens — he says **the moss has agreed to help**.

As Pax grows in power — especially if he becomes a **Warrior of the Elements** — the mystery should deepen. His elemental abilities may be Monk discipline. They may be Aasimar magic. They may be the result of his vision. They may be evidence that something ancient and natural has actually chosen him. The campaign should never fully resolve which, unless and until it serves the story.

## DM Note

Pax's three readings of himself — delusional, chosen, both — should stay live as long as possible. Give the world enough ambiguous evidence that neither the table nor Pax can settle it: plants that *might* lean toward him, healing that *might* be the moss, elemental power that *might* be celestial. Resist confirming or debunking the Green Revelation.

His confession in the Hall of Two Truths during the dream quest is a rich seam: a man who has reframed exile as purpose and sincerely believes he is the world's appointed hand. The scales don't weigh sincerity any more than they weigh context (cf. Sarric's confession). What does Pax confess when the world he speaks for is finally listening back?

The organization has three founding members. Their dynamic — who leads, who follows, who holds the others back or pushes them further — is worth establishing now that all three backstories are in hand. Pax is the one most likely to escalate, and the one least able to tell a sign from a symptom.
