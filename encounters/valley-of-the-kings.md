# The Valley of the Kings
## A D&D Encounter for 5 Players, Level 1

---

# OVERVIEW

The Valley of the Kings is the first major exploration site in the campaign. It contains four tombs, each dedicated to one of the ancient kings of the empire that once stood on the ground now occupied by Maalm. The empire collapsed thousands of years ago in a climate catastrophe — drought, famine, volcanic winter — and its final king, Amseh IV, sacrificed his own children to gods who did not answer. The tombs were looted of their valuables shortly after Maalm was founded. What remains are the walls, the paintings, the puzzles, the sarcophagi, and the dead.

The party has been sent here by the wizard to collect names. They do not yet know why he wants them.

---

# DRAMATIS PERSONAE

**Kethara (also called Farseh-ti)** — the wife of Amseh IV, now an ancient mummy who has remained in the valley for thousands of years caring for the lepers who shelter in Tomb 1. She has healing powers limited enough that she cannot cure leprosy but sufficient to ease suffering and extend life. She is not malevolent. She is grief made undead.

**The Lepers** — six individuals living in and around Tomb 1. They are not aggressive unless the party initiates violence. They are waiting for a supply shipment from a charity in the city. They are human beings.

**The Lions** — two cave lions living in Tomb 2. They are servants of the ancient gods and, if spoken to via Speak with Animals, are eloquent beyond any natural lion. A third lion waits in reserve in the darkness of the tomb and will emerge if combat begins.

**The Mummies** — the preserved remains of Amseh I through IV, sealed in their sarcophagi in the inner chambers of their respective tombs. They do not move. They do not speak. They are simply there, in the dark, in the silence of a very long time.

---

# THE MAP MECHANIC

The party carries a sending stone paired with a stone held by the wizard's associate. When a party member taps the sending stone against the location marked on the ancient map — the Valley of the Kings — they hear the wizard's voice:

**Read aloud — the wizard's sending stone message:**

*"You're standing in the valley of the ancient kings of the land we now know as Maalm. As you probably already know, shortly after Maalm was founded on the ancient ruins, the lord of Maalm cleaned out these tombs. The gods of the ancients are dead now, but there are other powers in this world than gods and stolen relics. I've sent you on this quest in search of names. Search the tombs, get into the inner burial chambers. Find what names you can and relay them to my associates using the sending stone. I don't expect you to find jewels, but know that I will pay you good prices for any jewels you may find on your journeys."*

---

# THE WALL LEDGERS — Reading the Economic Record

*A deployable beat for any tomb. Beside the religious paintings, every tomb's walls are ruled into columns of **trade and harvest figures** — the fullest economic history of the dead empire. A character who studies them (**DC 12 Intelligence**, or automatic for anyone who reads Ancient Egyptian) can follow the numbers. Comparing tombs tells a story the paintings don't.*

**Read aloud — reading the ledgers (Tombs 1 & 2, the prosperous kings):**

*"Between the gods and the grave-scenes, the walls are ruled into neat columns of figures — harvest tallies, ship manifests, tribute lists, kept like a merchant's book. Grain up the river. Guano by the boatload. And metal: ingots of **copper** out of Lonka, ingots of **tin** carried down from the western mountains, and — rarer, set apart, almost reverently — beads of golden **amber** from somewhere far to the south, past the edge of any map you know. Year after year the numbers climb. The kingdom is growing rich, and you can see exactly how: it sits at the center of a web of roads that reaches the whole world."*

**Read aloud — if they then read Tomb 3 (the warrior king):**

*"The same columns, the same careful hand — and then, partway down a wall, in the hundredth year of the king, everything changes. The grain figures fall off a cliff. The guano stops. The copper stops. The **tin stops.** The amber stops. The whole web of roads goes dark at once, in a single year. And where the trade tallies end, a new kind of figure begins — soldiers, spears, rations for armies. You are looking at the exact year the world ended, written in a bookkeeper's neat hand."*

**The payoff (DM):** the ledgers are the **Bronze-Age Collapse made legible.** Bronze needs *both* **copper** (from [Lonka](../lore/lonka-jotunheim.md)) **and tin** (from the [Suartleheim West](../lore/technology-and-trade.md) dwarf-mines), carried along sea-roads that all ran **through Lonka** — so when the [Bleeding Star](../lore/amseh-dynasty.md) shattered Lonka and cut the roads, **both halves of bronze died in one year,** and the weapons with them. A party that reads carefully grasps the catastrophe **economically,** not just mythically — and walks away with the names of trade-partners (Lonka; the western dwarf-mines; the amber-land in the deep south) that matter later.

# THE VALLEY ENTRANCE

## Read Aloud

*"The valley opens before you through a narrow canyon passage, and what you see when you emerge stops you in your tracks. On either side of the valley floor, carved directly from the living rock of the canyon walls, two colossal figures stand guard. They are enormous — perhaps sixty feet tall, their heads level with the canyon rim above. The figure on the left is a pharaoh, idealized and severe, wearing the double crown of upper and lower Egypt, a serpent diadem — the uraeus — coiled at his brow. He extends one arm forward, open-handed, as if claiming dominion over everything the hand can reach. The figure on the right is something else. From the waist up it is human, an Egyptian face of the same severe idealized beauty, arms extended in a gesture of cold regal judgment — one hand on its chest, one reaching outward. From the waist down it is a serpent, colossal coils disappearing into the rock of the canyon floor. Both figures have been here a very long time. The stone is weathered but intact. They are looking at you with the expressions of people who have seen everything and found most of it wanting."*

## If They Investigate the Statues

A DC 12 History check identifies these as the founding figures of the ancient empire — not gods, but kings who were worshipped as gods. The pharaoh figure wears a cartouche that can be read with a DC 14 Intelligence check or automatically by anyone who can read Ancient Egyptian: **Amseh**. A dynastic name, not an individual one. All four kings of this valley bore it.

The serpent-king figure bears no cartouche. It predates the dynasty. Whatever it was, it was here before the Amshehs built their tombs in this valley. They built around it.

A DC 10 Perception check at the base of the statues reveals the ancient map on the valley floor — a carved relief, partially worn, showing the coastline of the world as it was six thousand years ago. Three islands that exist today are absent. The northern sea is larger. In the upper right corner of the map, a tiny island with a serpent carved on it in detail disproportionate to its size — gold-scaled, lapis-blue, far more carefully rendered than anything else on the map.

# THE LEPER ENCOUNTER

*This encounter occurs at the entrance to Tomb 1. Run it before the players enter the tomb.*

## Setup

Six lepers live in and around the entrance to Tomb 1. They shelter here because the valley is otherwise empty of people and because something in the tomb eases their suffering slightly — they don't know it is Kethara. They are expecting a supply shipment from a charity in the city. If the party brought the supplies from the city gate, the lepers are immediately friendly. If the party did not bring supplies, the encounter begins with wariness and may escalate depending on how the party handles it.

## Read Aloud — Approaching Tomb 1

*"The first tomb entrance is cut directly into the valley wall. No grand facade — just a rectangular darkness in the pale stone, about six feet high. The hieroglyphs above the entrance are badly weathered. At the base of the entrance, someone has been living here. Scraps of cloth, the remains of small fires, crude clay vessels. The smell reaches you before anything else. Seven figures are visible — six huddled near the entrance and one slightly apart from the others, taller, more still. All of them are wrapped in linen."*

## The Lepers

They are not aggressive. They will not initiate violence. They wail, they beg, they are unwell. If the party brought supplies they are grateful and talkative. If not, they ask for anything — food, water, coin. If the party offers rat meat, their spokesperson responds:

*"We may have leprosy, but we are still human beings."*

Their spokesperson is an older woman whose eyes are sharp despite everything. She can tell the party:

- The inner room has a door covered in carved scarabs that move when pressed — the lepers don't touch it
- Someone tried to get through about a year ago, a well-dressed man with two guards. They heard screaming. The guards left without him.
- The valley has lions in the second tomb. The lepers consider the lions neighbors of a sort. They do not bother each other.
- The valley is old. Older than Maalm. The gods here are not the gods of Maalm.

If the party treated the lepers with genuine kindness — brought supplies, offered food without being asked, healed someone if they have that ability — she adds:

*"The walls inside show the journey of the dead. We have looked at them many times. There is a bird that flies up from the body. I used to think that was a sad thing. Now I think it might be the only good part."*

## Kethara

Kethara is the seventh figure — the tall still one slightly apart from the others. She is indistinguishable from the lepers until she isn't. DC 15 Perception or Medicine check to notice her unnaturally still breathing and the faint glow behind her wrappings.

She does not speak unless the party initiates violence against the lepers. If they do, see the Kethara Reveal below.

If the party is friendly and the spokesperson is talking, Kethara simply watches. If a player moves toward her specifically and addresses her, she turns to face them but does not speak. A DC 12 Insight check reveals she understands everything being said. A DC 14 Insight check reveals she is making a continuous effort not to react to something — grief, or something adjacent to it, held very carefully in check.

She will not reveal herself voluntarily. She has been hidden for a very long time.

## If Combat Begins

Use the stat block provided. Key tactical notes:

**Round 1:** Lepers move toward the party, wailing. Kethara hangs back. She uses Warden's Interception if a leper is attacked.

**Reveal trigger:** When the first leper drops to 0 HP, or if the party shows no sign of stopping, Kethara steps forward and uses Shepherd's Rebuke — free, no recharge roll needed on reveal.

## Read Aloud — Kethara Reveals Herself

*"The tallest of them goes still. Then, with a slowness that has nothing human in it, she raises her hands to the wrappings at her face.*

*The linen comes away in a single long strip — dry, ancient, the color of old teeth — and what is underneath is not a face so much as what remains of one. The skin has been drawn tight over the bone and hardened there, like leather left too long in the sun. Her cheeks have collapsed inward. Where there should be lips there is only a dark seam, cracked at the corners, pulled back to expose teeth that are intact and very white — wrong, somehow, amid the ruin of everything around them.*

*Her eyes are open. They are not empty. They are amber, faintly luminous, and they are looking directly at you with something that is not hunger and not hatred. It takes you a moment to name it.*

*It is grief."*

## Kethara's Curse

If the party kills or seriously harms the lepers, Kethara curses one party member — the one who struck first, or the one who dealt the most damage. The curse causes the target's Charisma score to drop by 1 each day. If their Charisma reaches 0, they die. The curse can be lifted by the dream quest reward — this is what the children of the dead merchant offer in exchange for shepherding their father's soul.

## After the Encounter

Whether the party fought or negotiated, Kethara returns to her stillness among the lepers once the immediate situation is resolved. She does not follow them into the tomb. She has not been inside Tomb 1 in a very long time.

# TOMB 1 — KHEPRI
## The Tomb of Amseh I — The Tomb of Transformation

*The oldest of the four tombs. Amseh I founded the dynasty. His tomb is about beginnings — the preparation of the dead, the departure of the soul, the first step of the journey.*

---

## Entering — Read Aloud

*"The corridor opens into a square chamber about thirty feet across. The ceiling is low — eight feet, maybe less. The walls are covered floor to ceiling in painted figures, colors still vivid despite the dust and age. In the center of the room, a stone door is set into the far wall. The door is covered entirely in carved scarab beetles — dozens of them, each slightly different, arranged in a grid. The room smells of old stone and something faintly sweet that you cannot identify."*

---

## If They Investigate the Room Generally

The floor is stone, worn smooth by centuries. No furniture, no treasure visible, no obvious traps. The only exits are the corridor behind them and the scarab door ahead. The faint sweet smell has no identifiable source.

---

## If They Investigate the Scarab Door

The door is solid stone with no hinges or handle. It is covered in a 5x5 grid of carved scarab beetles, each approximately the size of a fist. Each scarab is in a slightly different position — wings open or closed, facing left or right, legs raised or lowered, mandibles spread or closed.

Any scarab depresses slightly when touched and springs back. A single press does nothing.

**DC 10 Investigation:** Four of the scarabs are slightly more worn than the others. They have been pressed many times before. These are the correct four, but this reveals only that they are correct — not the order.

**DC 14 Investigation:** The worn scarabs correspond to specific positions that match four scarabs depicted in the wall paintings in sequence.

---

## The Puzzle — The Scarab Sequence

**The answer** is in the wall paintings. The paintings show Khepri rolling the sun across the sky — four key moments depicted with a scarab in a specific position at each moment.

The correct sequence:
1. Wings spread, facing right — the scarab launching the sun at dawn
2. Wings closed, facing right — the scarab pushing the sun upward
3. Wings closed, facing left — the scarab guiding the sun downward
4. Wings spread, facing left — the scarab releasing the sun at dusk

**The timer:** Once the first scarab is pressed, a soft grinding sound begins — sand falling inside the door mechanism. The players have 60 seconds to press all four in the correct order. Use a real timer. Tell the players you are using one when the grinding starts.

**Wrong scarab:** The floor on one side of the room tilts slightly — a 10 degree angle, enough to unsettle footing. Everyone makes a DC 10 Dexterity saving throw or falls prone. No damage. The floor resets after 10 seconds.

**Timer runs out:** The mechanism resets. The four worn scarabs spring back. They can try again immediately.

**Success:** A deep resonant click, then the sound of stone counterweights moving. The scarab door slides sideways into the wall.

---

## The Painting — Read Aloud Only If They Look at the Walls

*"The paintings cover every inch of the walls from floor to ceiling. The colors are muted with age — amber and teal and ivory rather than bright gold and blue — but the images are clear. You see a body lying on a table, priests bent over it with instruments in their hands, touching the face, the eyes, the mouth. Above the body, rising from its chest, a small bird with a human face looks upward. Four jars stand in a row below the table, each with a different carved lid. Two figures stand in a doorway watching, their arms full of offerings. Above all of this, enormous and dominating the upper wall, a great beetle with its wings spread wide pushes a golden disk — the sun — toward the horizon."*

**If they study further — DC 12 Perception or explicit careful looking:**

*"The beetle appears multiple times across the painting, each time in a slightly different position — wings spread at the far left of the wall, wings closed and pushing in the middle, wings closed and pulling near the right, wings spread again releasing at the far right. Four positions. Four moments in a single journey."*

**If they ask about specific elements:**

**The bird with the human face:** *"It rises upward from the body with a look of — not distress, exactly. More like surprise. As if it has just realized something."* This is the Ba — the soul of Amseh I, depicted in its classic Egyptian form as it departs the body.

**The four jars:** *"Each lid is carved with a different face. A human face. A jackal. A baboon. A falcon. They are arranged in a precise order below the preparation table."* These are the canopic jars of the Four Sons of Horus — Imsety (human, liver), Duamutef (jackal, stomach), Hapy (baboon, lungs), Qebehsenuef (falcon, intestines). Their names are inscribed on the jars in hieroglyphs, readable with a DC 10 Intelligence check or automatically by anyone who can read Ancient Egyptian.

**The priests:** *"One of them holds a small instrument — curved, like a miniature adze — and touches it to the lips of the mummified figure. The other two stand with their hands raised."* This is the Opening of the Mouth ceremony — the ritual that restores the dead person's senses for the afterlife.

**The family in the doorway:** *"They carry bread, flowers, a small vessel of liquid. Their faces are in profile the way all the figures are painted, but something in the line of their shoulders reads as grief."*

**The beetle above:** *"Above the doorway to the scarab puzzle, carved separately from the painting, a single hieroglyphic name: Khepri."*

---

## The Inner Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The short corridor beyond the scarab door opens into a smaller chamber. The air here is different — stiller, colder, with the quality of a space that has been sealed for a very long time. In the center of the room, a stone sarcophagus. It is plain by the standards of what you might imagine for a king — the riches that once surrounded it are gone, the niches in the walls are empty. The sarcophagus itself is carved with a figure in relief, arms crossed over its chest, eyes open and forward-facing. Above the figure, a cartouche. The walls of this chamber are undecorated. Just stone."*

**The Sarcophagus:**

The cartouche above the relief figure reads **Amseh I** in Ancient Egyptian hieroglyphs — DC 10 Intelligence or automatic for Ancient Egyptian readers.

The sarcophagus is sealed and extremely heavy. Opening it requires a combined DC 20 Strength check. Inside is a mummified body wrapped in ancient linen, perfectly preserved. Nothing else. The body is Amseh I.

**Names findable in this chamber:**
- Amseh I — sarcophagus cartouche
- Imsety, Duamutef, Hapy, Qebehsenuef — canopic jar inscriptions in the puzzle room
- Khepri — carved above the puzzle door
- The Ba — depicted but unnamed, as it is the soul of the king specifically

# TOMB 2 — RA
## The Tomb of Amseh II — The Tomb of the Sun

*The second tomb. Amseh II's reign was the dynasty's height — prosperity, trade, the great merchant routes. His tomb is about the journey itself, the solar barque crossing the night, the dangers beneath.*

---

## Exterior — Read Aloud

*"The second tomb is grander than the first — a proper entrance facade cut into the cliff, with a wide descending staircase leading down to a sealed stone door. The hieroglyphs here are better preserved. Above the door, carved in deep relief, a sun disk with wings spread across the full width of the entrance. The door itself stands open. Something forced it, recently — the stone seal that once closed it lies in pieces at the base of the stairs."*

---

## If They Investigate the Exterior

The broken seal was forced from inside, not outside — the stone is pushed outward. Something left, not broke in.

The stairs descend about fifteen feet. The air rising from below is warmer than the valley air and smells of animal — musky, large.

**DC 12 Perception** at the top of the stairs: a low rhythmic sound from below. Breathing.

---

## The Lions

Two cave lions have made the entrance chamber their den. They are not supernatural in appearance. But they are servants of the ancient gods, and if a party member casts Speak with Animals, they speak with the vocabulary and affect of beings who have been in this valley for a very long time.

If the party is quiet and careful — group DC 12 Dexterity (Stealth) — they can move through the entrance chamber without waking the lions. On a success they reach the puzzle room door undisturbed. On a failure one lion raises its head and watches. If they freeze and wait, it settles again. If they make another noise it stands.

If Speak with Animals is cast:

The lions speak in the most elevated register possible — formal, unhurried, faintly archaic. They are not hostile. They are assessing.

If the party is respectful: *"You smell of the living city. It has been some time since the living came here with anything other than fear or greed. Which are you?"*

If the party is rude or flippant: *"And who are you that I should bow so low?"*

They can tell the party:

- The tombs have been here longer than Maalm. The gods who built them are gone but the valley remembers.
- The lepers at the first tomb are under the protection of something old. The lions leave them alone.
- The puzzle rooms are not traps. They are tests. The old kings wanted only those worthy of their names to find them.
- They have not entered the inner chambers themselves. Some thresholds are not for lions.

If the party offers them food — rat meat, anything — the lions accept it with dignity. *"We have not eaten today. This is acceptable."*

If combat begins:

Use the Cave Lion stat block. A third lion emerges from the darkness of a side alcove at the end of Round 1 — it has been there the whole time, invisible in the shadow. The lions use Pack Tactics aggressively. They will not pursue a retreating party beyond the entrance chamber.

If the party fought the lepers before reaching this tomb:

Any surviving lepers have positioned themselves outside Tomb 2 while the party was inside Tomb 1. They attack when the party emerges. This is not organized — it is desperate and sad. They have clubs and their disease and their grief.

---

## The Entrance Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The staircase opens into a low rectangular chamber. The walls here are bare stone — no paintings, no carvings. Two large shapes are curled against the far wall, their breathing slow and deep. A door on the left wall stands ajar, leading to a second chamber where a faint light originates from somewhere above."*

---

## The Puzzle Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The second chamber is circular, about forty feet across. Twelve stone pillars stand in a ring around the perimeter, each carved with different symbols — figures, animals, a sun at different heights in the sky. In the center of the room stands a stone obelisk, chest high, its top carved to a point. A single shaft of light falls from a hole in the ceiling directly onto the obelisk, casting a sharp shadow across the floor toward one of the pillars. The room is otherwise dark."*

---

## If They Investigate the Room Generally

The light shaft comes from directly above — a narrow hole bored through approximately twenty feet of rock to the surface. Natural sunlight. Its angle shifts slowly as time passes — this is a functioning sundial.

The obelisk can be rotated. It sits in a shallow circular groove. Two people rotate it easily; one person requires a DC 10 Strength check.

---

## If They Investigate the Pillars

Each pillar is carved with a symbol representing an hour of the day and a scene showing what happens at that hour. Reading around the circle from the entrance: pre-dawn, dawn, early morning, mid-morning, late morning, **noon**, early afternoon, mid-afternoon, late afternoon, early evening, dusk, night.

The noon pillar — sixth from the entrance, directly opposite — shows the sun at its absolute zenith. Its carving is slightly more elaborate. **DC 10 Investigation** or careful examination: the noon pillar's base has a seam — it is a door, sealed, that opens when the obelisk shadow points to it.

The hieroglyphs around the walls — **DC 12 Intelligence (History)** or read aloud to the party: *"I am born each morning. I die each evening. Find the hour of my greatest power."*

---

## The Puzzle — Light and Shadow

**The answer:** Rotate the obelisk until its shadow falls on the noon pillar.

**Wrong pillars:** Each wrong pillar triggers a trap after five seconds. Visual warnings precede each — a grinding sound or hiss — giving a DC 12 Perception check to notice and a DC 12 Dexterity save to avoid:

- *Pre-dawn / Night:* Darkness — the light shaft closes for 30 seconds. Disorienting, not dangerous.
- *Dawn / Dusk:* Floor tilts slightly — DC 10 Dexterity save or slide toward the wall.
- *Morning / Afternoon:* Sand from ceiling vents — vision obscured, movement costs double for one round.
- *Evening:* Darts — DC 12 Dexterity save or 1d4 piercing damage.

**Success:** The seam at the noon pillar's base glows amber. A deep click. The pillar slides back, revealing the corridor to the inner chamber.

---

## The Painting — Read Aloud Only If They Look at the Walls

*"The paintings cover the curved wall between the pillars. A river runs through the entire composition, stretching from one end of the wall to the other. But the river is not water — it is stars. White star shapes painted above and below the river's surface in equal measure, as if the river flows through the middle of the night sky itself. On this river of stars, a wooden boat sails through absolute darkness. Several figures are aboard — each performing a different task. One rows. One manages a sail. Others watch the dark water on each side. One figure stands alone at the prow, larger than the others, facing forward into the darkness ahead. Beneath the boat, in the lower portion of the painting where the darkness is deepest, something vast and coiled is suggested — a shape so large it seems impossible, most of it hidden in the dark pigment, only the suggestion of scale visible."*

If they study further:

*"Along the very bottom of the wall, a row of twelve small panels runs the full circumference of the chamber. Each shows a different scene — different figures, different creatures, different landscapes. They follow a sequence, beginning in darkness and ending in light."*

If they ask about specific elements:

**The river of stars:** This is the Duat — the Egyptian underworld, depicted as the river the sun god Ra travels each night from sunset to dawn. The boat is his solar barque.

**The figure at the prow:** *"Larger than the others — whether that means more important or simply further forward, you cannot say. Facing away from the viewer, into whatever darkness the boat is sailing toward."* This is Ra.

**The shape beneath the boat:** *"The more you look at it the less certain you are that it is there at all. And then you look again and the scale of it — the suggestion of coils the size of city walls — makes you want to look somewhere else."* This is Apep — the serpent of chaos, enemy of Ra, the thing that waits in the darkness of Hour 7 to devour the sun. His name is inscribed in hieroglyphs at the base of the painting, readable with DC 10 Intelligence or automatic for Ancient Egyptian readers.

**The twelve panels:** *"The first shows figures in darkness, slumped, waiting. The last shows a sun disk rising, figures with arms raised. Between them — creatures, gates, a figure with a jackal head, a figure with an ibis head, water and fire and darkness and something that might be a great serpent being held at bay by many figures working together."*

---

## The Inner Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The chamber beyond the noon pillar is small and low-ceilinged. In the center, a sarcophagus, plainer than you might expect. The niches in the walls are empty — whatever was placed here is long gone. One wall, however, is painted floor to ceiling with a single continuous scene unlike anything in the outer chamber. It takes you a moment to understand what you are looking at."*

The Shipwrecked Sailor painting:

*"A great ship on dark water, dwarfed by waves. A single figure clinging to a piece of wood. An island — abundant with fruit and grain, birds and fish. Then something enormous approaching through the trees. The serpent that fills the right half of the painting is unlike Apep in the outer chamber — that one was chaos and darkness and scale. This one is different. Thirty cubits long, its body overlaid with gold, its color like lapis lazuli, and its expression — insofar as a serpent can have an expression — is one of curiosity rather than hunger. A small human figure stands before it. They are talking. There are more scenes below: gifts exchanged, a ship arriving, the human figure departing, the island behind him beginning to dissolve into waves."*

If they ask about the serpent: its name is not given in the painting. It is depicted as a prince, a benefactor, something ancient and benevolent in a tradition that otherwise treats serpents as enemies. A DC 14 History check: this is the Serpent of Punt — a figure from an ancient tale, ruler of an island of abundance, who helped a shipwrecked sailor return home.

**The Sarcophagus:**

The cartouche reads Amseh II. The sarcophagus is sealed. Inside, a mummified body. Nothing else.

**Names findable in this chamber:**
- Amseh II — sarcophagus cartouche
- Ra — depicted and named in puzzle room paintings
- Apep — named in hieroglyphs at base of puzzle room painting
- The Serpent of Punt — depicted but unnamed in inner chamber painting

# TOMB 3 — HAPI
## The Tomb of Amseh III — The Tomb of the Waters

*The third tomb. Amseh III was the warrior king — his reign defined by the invasion of the Sea Peoples, the bleeding star, the catastrophe that began the empire's long decline. His tomb is about the battle, the judgment that follows, and the silence of gods who watched both.*

---

## Exterior — Read Aloud

*"The third tomb entrance is at ground level, almost invisible — the hieroglyphs above it are completely worn away, and the entrance is half-buried in drifted sand. Someone has been digging it out recently. The excavation is rough, hasty — not the work of careful hands. Whoever did this wanted in quickly. The entrance beyond the excavated sand is clear."*

---

## If They Investigate the Exterior

The digging was done with simple tools — shovels and bare hands. Footprints of at least three people in sandals. Recent, within the last week.

No sign of whoever did the digging. They either went in and didn't come out, or left without being noticed.

**DC 14 Investigation:** One set of footprints is deeper on the way in — someone carrying something heavy. On the way out, those footprints are absent. Whatever they carried in, they didn't carry out.

---

## The Puzzle Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The entrance corridor is narrow and low — you need to crouch. It opens after about twenty feet into a larger chamber. The floor here is wet. Not flooded — just damp, a thin film of water covering the entire stone floor, reflecting your light back at you. The air is cooler here than outside and smells of the river — that specific green smell of the Nile in flood. Three stone basins line the left wall, each carved with a different symbol. Three more basins line the right wall. A seventh basin stands alone at the far end, larger than the others, carved with all three symbols combined. In the center of the room a large stone ewer sits on a low plinth, filled with water. The ceiling is low. A drain is set into the floor in the center of the room, currently sealed with a stone plug."*

---

## If They Investigate the Room Generally

The water on the floor is a quarter inch deep — seeping in from above, been here a long time, not rising. Yet.

The central ewer holds enough water to fill one basin completely or two partially. A second ewer near the entrance corridor — **DC 10 Perception** to spot — is empty and can carry additional water.

The drain is sealed and connected to the basin mechanism. It opens automatically when the correct sequence is completed.

The ceiling is seven feet high. When the water rises, this becomes relevant.

---

## If They Investigate the Basins

**Left wall, three basins — in order left to right:**
- **Shemu** — carved with a low river, cracked earth, the Nile at its driest
- **Akhet** — carved with a rising river, water touching the banks
- **Peret** — carved with fields and small plants emerging from receding water

**Right wall, three basins:** Same three symbols in a different, illogical order — deliberate decoys.

**Far wall, one basin:** All three symbols combined. The riddle carved above it: *"I give life and I take it. I rise without rain, I fall without drought. Feed me in the order I was born."*

---

## The Puzzle — The Sacred Waters

**The answer:** Fill the left wall basins in the correct annual order of the Nile's cycle — **Shemu first** (dry season, start of the agricultural year), then **Akhet** (the flood arrives), then **Peret** (waters recede, crops grow) — then fill the large combined basin at the far wall with remaining water.

Correct sequence: **Shemu → Akhet → Peret → the combined basin.**

**Wrong basin:** Water pours from ceiling vents. The floor level rises by six inches immediately. After one mistake: ankle deep. Two: knee deep. Three: waist deep, ceiling becoming relevant. Four: genuine emergency.

The water partially recedes when a correct basin is filled — mistakes are costly but recoverable.

**DC 12 Intelligence** to recognize that the left wall basins follow the correct annual sequence left to right. The right wall basins follow no logical order.

**Success:** The stone plug releases. The floor water drains rapidly. A door in the far wall beside the large basin clicks open.

---

## The Painting — Read Aloud Only If They Look at the Walls

*"Above the basins, the paintings cover the upper half of the walls. The dominant color is a deep green-black, the color of the Nile at flood. On the left wall: a battle at sea. Ships locked together, warriors struggling on the decks, the waves enormous and dark around them. Above the battle, in the sky, something burns — a star, bleeding light across the upper register of the painting, its tail longer than the ships below it. Around the edges of the painting, carved in tight hieroglyphic columns, text in two registers — a formal inscription above and something denser, more urgent below. On the right wall, the same battle from the shore — a king in a chariot directing his forces, his scale enormous compared to the soldiers around him, his arm extended toward the sea. Below him, along the base of the wall, heaped shapes that resolve into bodies when you look longer than you want to."*

If they read the hieroglyphic text — DC 12 Intelligence or automatic for Ancient Egyptian readers:

The formal inscription names the battle as the defense against the Sea Peoples — the invaders who came after the bleeding star. The king names himself: **Amseh III**. He invokes **Montu**, the god of war, and **Harakhte**, the horizon form of Ra.

The denser lower text is less formal — it has the quality of something written quickly, or under duress: *"In the year of the bleeding star, when the sky burned in the night and seven great thunders were heard throughout the land, rafts of skeletons washed upon our shore and in their wake came the invaders for the first time."*

If they ask about the bleeding star: A DC 14 History check yields nothing certain — this predates any records in living memory. The image is a comet or a meteor, enormous, depicted with the detail given to important things.

---

## The Inner Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The inner chamber is smaller than the others. The sarcophagus is here, in the center, sealed. But your attention goes immediately to the walls. Four figures dominate the painting on the far wall. Three are seated on thrones elevated above the scene — enormous, formal, their faces in the severe profile of judgment. The fourth stands to one side, slightly smaller, with the head of an ibis and a writing palette in its hands. It is writing. Below the three seated figures, a small human figure stands with its hands raised. It is not worshipping. It is being assessed."*

If they study the painting:

*"The three seated figures are not gods you recognize from anywhere in the other tombs. They are assessors — judges of some kind, each one slightly different. One has a serpent head. One has a jackal head. One has a face that is almost human but not quite. Below each throne, in vertical columns, their names: Neha-hau. Am-khaibitu. Hept-Shet. The ibis-headed figure to the side is Thoth — his name is carved beside him, the same figure from the outer chamber paintings, here in a different role. He is not judging. He is recording the verdict."*

**The Sarcophagus:**

The cartouche reads Amseh III. Sealed. Inside, a mummified body. Nothing else.

# TOMB 4 — OSIRIS
## The Tomb of Amseh IV — The Tomb of Judgment

*The last tomb. Amseh IV was the final king of the dynasty — the one who watched the empire starve, who sacrificed his children to gods who did not answer, who died in an unfinished tomb while his wife Farseh-ti survived him by remaining where she should not have. His tomb is the most elaborate and the most broken.*

---

## Exterior — Read Aloud

*"The fourth tomb is the most imposing of the valley — a proper temple facade cut into the cliff face, columns carved in relief on either side of a tall entrance, the lintel above carved with a procession of figures moving toward a central seated form. The entrance is sealed. A large stone door, fitted precisely into the frame, with no visible mechanism for opening it from outside. The stone around the edges of the door is blackened — not from fire exactly, but as if something very cold passed this way and left its mark on the rock."*

---

## If They Investigate the Exterior

The blackening is not soot — it is the stone bleached of color, a cold stain. **DC 14 Arcana:** residual energy from a powerful magical seal broken from the inside at some point in the distant past. Whatever was meant to keep things in — or out — is no longer active.

The door can be pushed open with a combined DC 15 Strength check. Two or more characters reduces this to DC 10.

The carved figures on the lintel show souls in procession approaching a seated form on a throne. The seated form holds a crook and a flail. Its crown is the Atef — tall, white, flanked by ostrich feathers.

---

## The Entrance Corridor — Read Aloud

*"The corridor beyond the door is wider than the others — wide enough for three people to walk abreast. The walls here are painted even in the corridor, figures processing toward the inner chamber in long rows on each side. The ceiling above is dark with age. The air is very still. At the end of the corridor, perhaps sixty feet ahead, a chamber opens — larger than anything you have seen in the other tombs, faintly lit from scattered points of light in the ceiling."*

---

## The Puzzle Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The chamber is large — perhaps sixty feet across, with a ceiling high enough that the darkness above swallows the top of the walls. In the center of the room stands a set of scales, golden, enormous — each pan large enough to hold a person. One pan holds a single white feather. The other pan holds an obsidian bowl. Around the room, forty-two stone tablets are scattered — some upright, some fallen, some stacked against the walls, each engraved with text. The walls themselves are painted floor to ceiling in a composition that makes the room feel like it continues outward in every direction."*

---

## If They Investigate the Scales

The scales are functional — the pans move freely and balance perfectly when both are empty. The white feather is real, impossibly pristine for a tomb this old.

The obsidian bowl is fixed to the pan — it cannot be removed. It rejects impersonal objects. Placing a coin or a rock tips the scales but nothing else happens.

---

## If They Investigate the Tablets

Forty-two tablets. Each engraved with a confession in hieroglyphs and below that, in the common tongue:

*"I have stolen."*
*"I have lied."*
*"I have caused pain to another."*
*"I have taken what was not mine."*
*"I have spoken words that destroyed."*
*"I have remained silent when I should have spoken."*
*"I have acted from anger and called it justice."*

And so on — forty-two, covering the range of human wrongdoing from mundane to profound.

**DC 10 Investigation:** The tablets are a framework, not an exhaustive list. A sin not listed might still be confessable by speaking it aloud rather than placing a tablet.

---

## The Puzzle — The Weighing

**The mechanic:** Each party member chooses one tablet representing something their character has genuinely done and places it in the obsidian bowl. The scales register honesty. When a true confession is placed, the scales shift and then settle back toward balance. A door in the far wall — currently invisible, camouflaged in the painting — opens one lock for each honest confession. Five characters, five confessions, five locks.

**False confession — psychic damage:** If a player places a tablet for something their character has not done, the scales tip violently and a pulse of psychic energy radiates from the bowl — 1d6 psychic damage to the character who placed it, no save. The tablet is ejected. The scales resettle. They can try again.

Each character who experiences the psychic damage has a brief involuntary vision of a moment from their past they would prefer not to think about. Describe the sensation rather than the content — an unwanted memory surfacing suddenly, clearly, and receding.

**A character whose sin isn't on any tablet:** They can name their sin aloud. If true, the bowl glows faintly and registers it without a tablet.

**If they are stuck:** A DC 10 Insight check gives a character the clear sense that the room is waiting for something to be acknowledged rather than performed.

**The door:** When all five confessions are accepted, a seam appears in the far wall painting — a door-shaped outline previously invisible. It swings inward.

---

## The Painting — Read Aloud Only If They Look at the Walls

*"The paintings in this chamber are the most elaborate you have seen — and the most unsettling, though not in a way you can immediately explain. The composition is perfectly symmetrical, centered on the far wall where something enormous is seated on a throne. The figure wears a tall crown flanked by feathers. It holds a crook and a flail crossed over its chest. Behind its throne, a curtain of deep blue — painted so carefully that it seems almost to move. This figure's eyes are open and forward-facing, which is unusual — every other figure in every other tomb you have visited is shown in profile. This one looks directly out of the painting. Directly at you. Before the throne, a set of scales — a jackal-headed figure tends them. On one pan, a feather. On the other, a heart. To one side, a figure with the head of an ibis records on a palette. Beside the scales, something waits. Part lion. Part hippopotamus. Its head is the head of a crocodile. It is not looking at the scales. It is looking at the same place the seated figure is looking. At you. Along both side walls, rows of smaller figures are seated in judgment. You count them without meaning to. Forty-two."*

If they study further:

*"The painting continues beyond the throne. Behind the blue curtain — visible only because the painter chose to show what lies beyond it — a landscape opens. Fields of impossible green. Water that catches a light that has no source. Small figures moving through it with the unhurried quality of people who have nowhere particular to be and all the time there is. It occupies perhaps one tenth of the total composition. Everything else is the judgment that precedes it."*

If they ask about specific figures:

**The seated figure:** *"You cannot look at it for very long comfortably. Not because it is frightening. Because it looks back."* This is Osiris — his name carved in hieroglyphs beside the throne, readable DC 10 or automatic for Ancient Egyptian readers.

**The blue curtain:** *"The painter used more pigment here than anywhere else in the composition."*

**The jackal-headed figure:** Anubis — named in hieroglyphs beside him.

**The ibis-headed figure:** Thoth — named in hieroglyphs beside him.

**The creature beside the scales:** *"It has the patience of something that has never once been disappointed."* This is Ammit — named in hieroglyphs. Part lion, part hippopotamus, part crocodile. She devours the hearts of the unworthy.

**The two figures flanking the throne:** Isis and Nephthys, sisters of Osiris, named in hieroglyphs.

**The figure at the corner of the throne:** Selket — scorpion crown, named in hieroglyphs.

**The forty-two figures on the side walls:** The Assessors of Ma'at. Their individual names are inscribed beside each one in hieroglyphs but would take considerable time to record — an hour of careful work to transcribe all forty-two.

---

## The Inner Chamber — Read Aloud

*"The inner chamber stops you at the threshold. Not because of what is in it — but because of what is not. The other tombs were stripped of treasure but finished. This one was not finished. The paint on the walls is incomplete — raw limestone visible where the artists stopped working, outlines sketched in red ochre but never filled in, pigment applied only to the central composition and fading to bare stone at the edges. Scaffolding marks in the stone show where the painters stood. A section of ceiling above you has plaster applied but nothing drawn on it.*

*In the center of the room, a sarcophagus. Beside it, not in front of it, a second inscription carved into the wall at eye level — smaller than the cartouche on the lid, more careful, as if someone took time with it that they didn't have.*

*But your eyes go to the base of the walls first. Arranged in a row, so carefully placed they could not be accidental, seven small rectangular shapes. Child-sized. Each one slightly different. Each one sealed. They are painted in blue — still vivid, still saturated, in a chamber where everything else has faded. Someone used the best pigment they had for those."*

If they look at the unfinished painting:

*"The central composition shows a kneeling figure — a king, identifiable by his crown, which is slightly askew — with his hands raised toward a row of empty thrones above him. The thrones are elaborate. No gods sit in them. The empty seats are the most important thing in the image."*

If they ask about the seven coffins:

They are sealed and small. A DC 12 Medicine check confirms they are sized for children. They have not been disturbed. There is nothing more to know about them from the outside.

If they ask about the unfinished quality:

*"The ghost of an outline in red ochre marks a figure that was never painted. A section of ceiling where the plaster was applied but nothing was drawn. The tomb was not abandoned — someone kept working until they couldn't. But they ran out of something. Time, or people, or the will to continue."*

**The Main Inscription — if they read the hieroglyphic columns:**

DC 12 Intelligence or automatic for Ancient Egyptian readers:

*"Oh gods of my father, why have you forsaken me? Your voices which spoke so loud are silent. The day shines bright over the Land, but for me the day is black. The bright day has become a dark day. Tears, lament, anguish and despair are lodged within me. I shall be the last king of this land. I offer you my children as penance, yet still you are silent. I have no heirs. The people starve and the land is dry and barren."*

**The second inscription beside the sarcophagus — smaller, more careful:**

DC 10 Intelligence or automatic for Ancient Egyptian readers:

Two names. A cartouche for **Amseh IV**. Beside it, not above it, not below it — beside it, as an equal — a second inscription: **Farseh-ti**.

**The Sarcophagus:**

The cartouche reads Amseh IV. Sealed. Inside, a mummified body. Nothing else. The tomb was stripped like the others — but in this chamber there was less to strip. It was never finished.

**Names findable in this chamber:**
- Amseh IV — sarcophagus cartouche
- Farseh-ti — wall inscription beside the sarcophagus
- Osiris — puzzle room painting
- Anubis — puzzle room painting
- Thoth — puzzle room painting
- Isis — puzzle room painting
- Nephthys — puzzle room painting
- Selket — puzzle room painting
- Ammit — puzzle room painting
- The Forty-Two Assessors — puzzle room walls, require one hour to transcribe fully
