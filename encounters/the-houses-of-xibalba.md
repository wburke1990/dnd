# The Houses of Xibalba

## A D&D Encounter — Design Note

*A Maya-underworld descent, built on the same bones as [The Valley of the
Kings](valley-of-the-kings.md): a sequence of themed trial-rooms, each with
one clean puzzle mechanic, a wall-scene that carries the mythology, and
names to be found and carried back. Where the Valley was four tombs read in
daylight, this is six houses read in the dark, one after another, with no way
back up until they are finished.*

> `[OPEN]` **Placement & level.** Not yet sited in Nila and not yet leveled.
> The descent structure (rivers, then a name-council, then six houses in a
> row, then the ballcourt) wants a party that can take a beating and think
> under a timer — a mid-to-high site, not a first one. Where in the world
> the mouth of the descent sits is a separate decision. Left open on purpose.

---

# THE SHAPE OF IT

The Egyptian tombs were a museum: the dead were dead, the puzzles were locks,
the danger was incidental. Xibalba is the opposite. The place is inhabited.
It is run by lords who invite the living down to play a game and then kill
them, and it has been doing this a long time. Everything in it is a test set
by someone who is watching.

Four load-bearing pieces, in order:

1. **The Descent** — three rivers and a crossroads. A deduction puzzle, no combat.
2. **The Council of Lords** — a room of seated figures, most of them fakes.
   Learn the real ones' names or be caught out. This is the names hook, turned
   into a puzzle in its own right.
3. **The Six Houses** — the main trial. One room, one mechanic, one night each.
4. **The Ballcourt** — the set-piece finish, and the only way the living have
   ever walked back out.

# 1. THE DESCENT — The Rivers and the Crossroads

## Read Aloud

*"The stair ends at water. Three rivers cross the path one after another. The
first is thick and moving and the color of a wound. The second runs pale
yellow and the smell reaches you before the edge does. The third is not water
at all — the whole surface is scorpions, backs and tails, packed shoulder to
shoulder from bank to bank. Past the third river the path splits four ways.
One road is red. One is white. One is yellow. One is black, and the black
one is the only one wide enough to walk without turning sideways."*

## The Puzzle — The Four Roads

The black road is the easy one, the open one, the one that wants to be
walked. It leads to the Lords, and to being made a fool of on arrival.

- **DC 12 Investigation** at the crossroads: the red, white, and yellow roads
  are worn — foot-packed, travelled. The black road's surface is clean. Nothing
  living has walked it. Things have been *led* down it.
- **DC 14 Insight / History**, or reading the glyphs cut at the fork: the
  colored roads are the four directions, and the dead take them home. The black
  road is not a direction. It is the throat of the place.
- A party that takes a colored road reaches the council chamber **unannounced**
  and can approach the Lords from the side — the difference between walking in
  on the trick and walking into it.

**If they take the black road anyway:** it delivers them straight in front of
the seated council, in full view, with no chance to scout — and the room
below runs against them from the first word.

*(Design: this is the Egyptian obelisk-shadow beat rebuilt as a social
approach — a wrong answer that isn't lethal, just costly. It sets up Room 2.)*

# 2. THE COUNCIL OF LORDS — Learning the Names

The heart of the Maya story, and the cleanest possible marriage to this
campaign's names thesis: the way you survive the Lords of Xibalba is by
**knowing their names before they tell you.**

## Read Aloud

*"The chamber is long and lined on both sides with seated figures, a dozen or
more, upright on stone benches, dressed as lords — feathered, jeweled, still.
At the far end two thrones sit higher than the rest. Every figure faces the
door. None of them has moved since you came in. None of them has spoken."*

## The Setup

Most of the figures are **wooden manikins** — decoys, dressed as lords, seated
among the real ones to catch newcomers out. The custom of the place is that a
visitor is expected to walk the line and greet each lord by name and title.
Greet a manikin, or guess a name wrong, and the whole council knows you came
down blind — and the houses that follow are set to the hardest setting.

There are two real rulers on the high thrones — **One Death** and **Seven
Death** — and a bench of lesser lords whose names are the names of sicknesses:
**Scab Stripper**, **Blood Gatherer**, **Pus Demon**, **Jaundice Demon**,
**Bone Staff**, **Skull Staff**, **Sweepings Demon**, **Stabbing Demon**.

## The Puzzle — Sort the Living from the Wood

The answer is not to guess. It is to make the room give itself away.

- **The bite.** A party that thinks to send something small and biting down the
  line first — a conjured insect, a familiar, a mage hand flicking each figure —
  learns everything. Wood does not flinch. The living lords do, and when they
  do they curse and say each other's names out loud: *"Quiet — is it your foot,
  Blood Gatherer?"* One pass, and the party has the real ones and their order.
- **Reading it cold.** No insect? **DC 15 Perception** across the benches: the
  manikins don't breathe, don't sweat, and the paint at their joints has cracked.
  The living lords are damp and the jewelry rises and falls. Slower, per-figure,
  but it works.
- **The greeting.** Walk the line and name only the living, in order, skipping
  the wood. Do it clean and the Lords are — put out. They expected fresh meat.
  The houses run at their normal setting, which is merely deadly.

**Names findable here:** One Death, Seven Death, and the eight sickness-lords
above. These are the marquee names of the whole descent — the ones worth
carrying back.

*(Design: this is the "collect names" hook made adversarial. In the Valley,
names were lying on the walls to be transcribed. Here the names defend
themselves, and taking them is the puzzle.)*

# 3. THE SIX HOUSES — The Main Trial

Six sealed rooms, run one per night, no exit until each is passed. This is the
four-tombs engine with two more chambers and a clock: the Lords expect the
house to kill you by morning, and morning is when they open the door to check.

Each house is **one mechanic**. None is a fair fight — each is beaten by a
trick, an item, or a bargain rather than by damage. That is the tonal signature
of the place: brute force is the losing move every time.

### Dark House — *the light that must survive the night*

Total dark. Each character is handed a **lit torch and a lit cigar** and told
plainly: keep them burning all night, and hand them back **whole and unburnt**
at dawn. The two demands can't both be met by burning them.

- **The trick:** fake the fire. A red glow on the torch that isn't flame — a
  macaw's tail-feather, a *dancing lights*, colored cloth over a cold source —
  and a firefly or a mote of light pinched to each cigar's tip. Return them
  uncharred at dawn and the house is beaten.
- Burn them for real and they're gone by first light; the Lords collect the
  debt in the ballcourt. **DC 13 Arcana / Survival** to land on a cold-light
  dodge if the table is stuck.

### Razor House — *the blades that want feeding*

The room is full of obsidian blades that move on their own, drifting, turning,
closing on warmth.

- **The trick:** they can be **spoken to and bargained with** (Speak with the
  blades via any talking-to-objects effect, or simply addressed aloud with a
  **DC 14 Persuasion**). Promise them the flesh of animals — the meat of every
  beast, instead of the party's — and the blades go still and lie down.
- Fight or dodge them and it's a save every round (**DC 14 Dex, 2d6 slashing**)
  until someone thinks to *talk*.

### Cold House — *hail indoors*

Wind, hail, and a cold that climbs toward killing. A **DC 13 Constitution save**
each round; failure is a level of exhaustion, and the house is counting on the
party to still the fire and freeze before dawn.

- **The trick:** keep the fire in. Block the vents, burn what's here, huddle
  and share warmth — any deliberate plan to hold heat shuts the save off. The
  house is beaten by refusing to let it work, not by enduring it.

### Jaguar House — *the hungry room*

Packed with jaguars, lean and starving, and the door won't open till dawn.

- **The trick:** feed them something that isn't you. Scatter **bones** across
  the floor (there are plenty — this house has eaten before) and the jaguars
  turn on the bones and each other. A party that walks in without a plan to
  redirect the hunger is the meal.

### Fire House — *the furnace*

A single sealed room of roaring flame, roof to floor.

- **The trick:** it's a room to be *outlasted*, not crossed — press low where
  the air is breathable, ration movement, take the burn (**DC 14 Con, 2d6 fire**
  per round of exposure) and survive to dawn rather than trying to reach a door
  that isn't there. The Lords expect the party to panic into the flame.

### Bat House — *keep your head down*

The last and worst. The dark is full of **death-bats** — knife-nosed, fast,
drawn to anything that lifts up out of cover. And somewhere in it is the great
bat, **Camazotz**, who takes heads.

- **The trick:** stay down and stay closed up. Wait out the night sealed inside
  armor, a shell, a *tiny hut*, whatever the party has. The **one** fatal
  mistake is lifting your head to look — to check if it's dawn yet, to see if
  the bats have stopped. In the story that's exactly the mistake that gets made,
  and it's how Hunahpu loses his head to the ballcourt. Telegraph the temptation
  and let a player make the call.

**Names findable across the houses:** Camazotz (Bat House). The houses are
otherwise about surviving, not transcribing — the names live in Room 2 and the
ballcourt.

# 4. THE BALLCOURT — The Finish

The reason any of this exists. The Lords summon the living down to Xibalba to
**play the ballgame** — and to lose it, and be killed. The party's forebears in
this story went down, lost, and stayed. Winning the descent means turning the
game around on the ones who run it.

## The Game

A stone court, sloped walls, a heavy rubber ball that can't be touched with
hands. The Lords play to maim, and cheat — the ball is theirs and it bites. This
is the campaign's chance for a genuine sport set-piece: contested rolls, court
positions, a crowd of the dead in the stands.

> `[OPEN]` **How to statline the game.** Wants its own pass — probably a
> skill-challenge ladder (Athletics / Acrobatics / a called shot) against the
> Lords' cheats rather than initiative-order combat. Flagged for a later build.

## The Only Way Out

The Maya answer, and the ending worth stealing whole: the Twins don't win by
scoring. They win by a **trick the Lords can't resist.** They let themselves be
killed, come back, and go about the underworld as a pair of ragged performers
doing a miracle — sacrificing a thing and reviving it, sacrificing *each other*
and reviving. Word reaches the thrones. The Lords, delighted, demand to be
sacrificed and revived themselves.

So the Twins sacrifice One Death and Seven Death — and simply don't bring them
back.

That is the shape of the exit: not a bigger hit than the Lords can take, but a
bargain the Lords walk into because they can't imagine being fooled. A party
that reaches the thrones by out-thinking, not out-hitting, ends the descent the
way the story does — by getting the two names at the top to kneel of their own
accord.
