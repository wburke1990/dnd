# The Wisdom Duel

## A D&D Encounter — Design Note

*A Norse riddle-contest for [Musleheim](../lore/musleheim.md), the fire-realm.
The opponent is a fire giant — an eldjötunn old enough to have watched the
comet build the island and poison it. It out-knows any mortal alive, so the
duel cannot be won on knowledge. It is won by the party changing the rules.*

*Built to answer one design problem: a riddle contest must not become a test of
who did the reading. Every gate here is either **solved by reasoning**,
**won by invention**, or **backstopped by a skill check** — never by recall.*

> `[OPEN]` **Stakes.** Left open on purpose — set them to whatever the party
> needs from the giant when this drops in (passage past it, a name it holds, a
> thing it took, a way down to the drowned capital). The wager only needs to
> **escalate each round**; the rest of the design doesn't care what it is.

---

# HOW IT WORKS — Three Lanes to Win

The contest is a back-and-forth of questions. The giant asks; the party may
ask back. It ends when one side can neither answer nor change the subject.
There is never a single knowledge bottleneck, because there are three ways to
take a round:

**1. Solve it.** The giant poses a riddle whose answer is hidden *inside the
clue* — describable, figure-out-able, no myth knowledge required (bank below).
The party confers and answers. Stuck? A **DC 13 History or Arcana check buys
one nudge** — so a well-read player *helps*, but a clever cold guess wins
without them. Anyone at the table can call the answer; it's a group puzzle, not
one person's spotlight.

**2. Turn it around.** On the party's turn they can pose *their own* riddle
back. Stump the giant and they take the round. This lane is pure invention —
zero recall — and it's usually where a good table has the most fun. The giant
is ancient and has never left this island: a riddle built from the surface
world, from a city it's never seen, from the party's own lives, can land where
a classic one wouldn't. Reward a clever riddle automatically; for a
shaky one, let the giant make an **Intelligence check** to crack it.

**3. Change the rules — the seeded question.** See below. This is the only
lane that reliably *ends* the duel against something this old, and it's the
one that rewards paying attention in the world.

---

# RUNNING IT — The Ladder

Don't run it as sudden death. Run a wager that escalates:

- Each round the loser gives up a piece of the stake — a memory, a year off
  their life, a true name, a finger, whatever the wager is. Physical or
  not, it should *hurt a little more each round*.
- Track it openly. The tension is watching the pile grow, not a single
  coin-flip miss.
- The duel ends when one side is out of lanes: can't solve, can't stump,
  can't change the subject. A party that keeps all three lanes alive can
  fence with the giant for as long as the table's enjoying it.

**Keep it off the trivia rail.** Two rules hold the line:
- The giant never asks a *fact* ("name Odin's spear"). Only riddles whose
  answer follows from the words. If you improvise one, make sure a smart
  stranger could get it.
- Every knowledge gate has the **DC 13 check** backstop. Forgetting a detail
  is never a wall — it's a die roll and a nudge.

**Whole table.** The giant asks the party, not a player. They confer out loud,
in character, and anyone answers. If one voice dominates, have the giant
demand the *next* answer come from someone who hasn't spoken.

# THE WHISPER, REBUILT — The Seeded Question

In the myth, Odin ends every wisdom duel the same way: he stops asking riddles
and asks the one question only he could answer — *what did I whisper in my dead
son's ear?* — and the contest breaks, because it was never winnable on
knowledge. Whoever will step outside the game wins it.

Keep the move, drop the literal line. The party's version is a question **only
they could ask** — and you **seed the answer earlier in the site** so a
paying-attention table has it in hand:

- A **name they collected** somewhere in Musleheim that the giant lost, or was
  never told, or has spent centuries trying to remember.
- A **fact from their own history** the giant cannot have witnessed, because it
  happened on the far side of the sea it's never crossed.
- Something the giant **buried and assumes is gone** — that the party dug up
  three rooms ago.

Ask it and the giant can't answer, can't stump, can't change the subject. All
three lanes close on it at once. Back the recall with the **DC 13 check** so a
half-remembered seed still surfaces. This is the win that costs nothing but
attention — the opposite of a trivia test.

*(Design: this is why the giant is ancient. Its age is the reason knowledge
can't beat it — and the reason a small human specific is the blade that can.)*

# THE RIDDLE BANK

*The giant's questions. Every answer is reasoning, not recall. Several are
re-skinned to the fire-realm (marked ★); the last two are plants — see notes.*

1. **The cow.** *"Four of it hang. Four of it walk. Two point the way ahead.
   Two watch for the dogs. And one trails behind, most often dirty."*
   — Udder, legs, horns, eyes, tail.

2. **The spider.** *"It waits by the door on eight legs and four eyes, and it
   carries its knees higher than its back."*

3. **Fog.** *"It rolls over the ground and swallows the water and the trees in
   front of it. It fears only the wind. It fears no man. And it makes war on
   the sun."*

4. **Dew.** *"Yesterday I drank a drink that was not ale, not mead, not water,
   not anything brewed, and no cup held it — and I went away with my thirst
   gone."*

5. **The anchor. ★** *"Smiths made its iron bones. It travels always toward a
   land it will never reach. The harder the sea pulls, the deeper it bites and
   the stiller the ship lies."*

6. **The sow and her litter.** *"It crosses the field as one. Yet it has ten
   tongues, twenty eyes, and forty feet."* — A sow with nine piglets. (Pure
   arithmetic — no lore.)

7. **Ale.** *"It loosens the tongues of men and unties their secrets. In the
   same breath it knots their speech and drops them where they stand."*

8. **Fire. ★** *"Feed it and it grows. Starve it and it dies. It eats the
   forest and it drinks the wind, and to touch what it has kissed is to be
   burned."*

9. **Ice.** *"A bridge no smith laid. It carries men and horses over the river
   in the cold months, and drowns them in the warm."*

10. **Iron. ★** *"Dug black out of the mountain. Drowned in fire until it runs
    like water. Beaten while it throws sparks. It comes out able to cut the
    hand that made it."*

11. **The egg.** *"A house with no door and no window. White walls, and gold
    shut inside. To reach the gold you must break the house."*

12. **A shadow.** *"It walks when you walk and stops when you stop. It is tall
    in the morning and short at noon. No blade has ever cut it."*

13. **A name.** *"A man owns it, and everyone else uses it more than he does.
    It is his from his first day, it cost him nothing, and it outlasts him."*
    — *Plant.* Seeds the campaign's names thesis and foreshadows Lane 3: the
    duel is about to be decided by a name.

14. **The comet. ★** *"It comes without feet and returns without being called.
    It raised this island out of the sea, and it poisoned it. The old ones
    kept count of the years between its comings."* — *Plant.* Only something
    that has seen the comet's passes could pose this; it marks the giant's age
    out loud, which is the whole reason Lane 3 works.

*Poser's note: riddles 13 and 14 are also the two the giant is proudest of, and
the two a player can turn around on it — a party that answers "a name" and then,
rounds later, asks the giant a question that hinges on a name it lost, is
playing the duel the way it's meant to be played.*

# THE OPPONENT

A fire giant / eldjötunn of Musleheim — old enough to remember the
[Muspel empire](../lore/the-muspel-empire.md) before the last comet pass
drowned Cartaga. It doesn't fight the party; it wagers with them. It is
courteous, patient, and certain it cannot lose, because in a contest of
knowledge it cannot. Statline it as a talker, not a brawler — the duel is the
encounter. If the party tries to skip the game and swing, it is a fire giant
and the game was the mercy.
