# Homeric Epithets Across Translations — Inspiration Reference

*Real-world reference / inspiration (not in-world canon). Homer's* Iliad *and*
Odyssey *in English translation. Homer's poems are built out of repeated
formulas — fixed phrases reattached to the same person, object, or moment every
time it appears. The singer used them as building blocks in live performance.
This file collects the most famous ones and how the major translators handle
them, as a pattern to imitate for recurring in-world phrasing.*

**Why it's here:** the campaign already runs heavily on sea and fleet material
([ships and sea power](../lore/ships-and-sea-power.md), [the floating
fleet](../lore/floating-fleet.md)) and on [myth and poem as literal
truth](../lore/myth-and-poem-as-literal-truth.md). The Homeric formula is the
working example of a culture whose poetry *is* its record-keeping — a fixed
phrase that arrives unchanged every time, because changing it would be changing
the record.

**Will's verdict so far:** Fitzgerald for the *Odyssey* — "fingertips of rose,"
and the Muse singing *in* and *through* the poet. On the *Iliad*'s opening line
specifically: Fagles, Alexander, and Pope — the three who put the wrath-word
first and make the invocation wait. That's a call on that one line, not on
those translations as a whole.

## The Odyssey

### The dawn formula

Homer repeats one line, unchanged, roughly twenty times: *êmos d' êrigeneia
phanê rhododaktylos Êôs*. How a translator treats that repetition is their
whole philosophy in miniature.

| Translator | Rendering |
|---|---|
| **Fitzgerald** | "When Dawn spread out her fingertips of rose" |
| Fagles | "When young Dawn with her rose-red fingers shone once more" |
| Lattimore | "When the young Dawn showed again with her rosy fingers" |
| Wilson | refuses a fixed formula — re-renders it fresh each time |
| Green | "When Dawn appeared, early risen and rosy-fingered" |
| Butler (prose, public domain) | "Now when the child of morning, rosy-fingered Dawn, appeared" |

Fitzgerald turns the epithet into a verb — dawn *does* something, the light
unfurls. Lattimore repeats his version identically every time, on purpose: the
sameness is the meaning. Wilson treats that sameness as the thing a modern ear
can no longer hear, and trades constancy for freshness.

### The opening line — *polytropos*

One Greek word, "of many turns," forces every translator to decide who
Odysseus fundamentally is.

| Translator | Rendering |
|---|---|
| **Fitzgerald** | "Sing in me, Muse, and through me tell the story" / "that man skilled in all ways of contending" |
| Fagles | "Sing to me of the man, Muse, the man of twists and turns" |
| Lattimore | "Tell me, Muse, of the man of many ways" |
| Wilson | "Tell me about a complicated man" |
| Butler (prose) | "Tell me, O Muse, of that ingenious hero who travelled far and wide after he had sacked the famous town of Troy" |

Fitzgerald alone makes the poet a vessel — the Muse sings *in* and *through*
him rather than being commanded. The Greek (*ándra moi énnepe, Moûsa*) is
grammatically an order addressed to the Muse, so Fitzgerald is the least
literal of the four here. He trades word-level fidelity for fidelity to how the
poetry was actually made: the Parry–Lord work on oral composition found singers
who understood the song as coming through them from the goddess.

### Other Odyssey formulas

| Formula | Fitzgerald | Others |
|---|---|---|
| *oînops póntos* — the sea | "the wine-dark sea" | nearly everyone keeps it; Lattimore sometimes "the wine-blue water" |
| *glaukôpis Athênê* | "grey-eyed Athena" | Fagles "bright-eyed Athena"; Lattimore "gray-eyed Athene"; Wilson varies; Butler Latinizes to "Minerva" |
| *épea pteróenta* | "winged words" | kept by nearly everyone |
| Odysseus, general | "the great tactician," "master mariner," "raider of cities" | — |
| Telemachus (*pepnyménos*) | "clear-headed Telémakhos" | — |

Fitzgerald uses Greek-style spellings throughout — Telémakhos, Kírkê, Kalypso,
Alkínoös, Krónos — where others Latinize.

## The Iliad

### The openings

The Greek opens on *mênin* — wrath — and that word is the key to the whole
poem; in antiquity the epic was sometimes called simply the *Mênis*. But Greek
grammar makes it the object of "sing," which in natural English wants to come
later. Every translator has to choose.

| Translator | Opening |
|---|---|
| **Fagles** | "Rage—Goddess, sing the rage of Peleus' son Achilles" |
| **Alexander** (2015) | "Wrath—sing, goddess, of the ruinous wrath of Peleus' son Achilles" |
| **Pope** (1715) | "Achilles' wrath, to Greece the direful spring / Of woes unnumber'd, heavenly goddess, sing" |
| Fitzgerald | "Anger be now your song, immortal one, / Akhilleus' anger, doomed and ruinous" |
| Lombardo | "Rage: / Sing, Goddess, Achilles' rage, / Black and murderous" |
| Lattimore | "Sing, goddess, the anger of Peleus' son Achilleus / and its devastation" |
| Wilson (2023) | "Goddess, sing of the cataclysmic wrath / of great Achilles" |
| Mitchell (2011) | "The rage of Achilles—sing it now, goddess, sing through me" |
| Butler (prose) | "Sing, O goddess, the anger of Achilles son of Peleus" |

**Wrath first:** Fagles, Alexander, Pope, Fitzgerald, Lombardo.
**Invocation first:** Lattimore, Wilson, Butler, Mitchell.

Pope belongs in the first group, not the second — his couplet opens on
"Achilles' wrath" and pushes "heavenly goddess, sing" to the end of line two.
That is the most aggressive wrath-first construction on the list.

Note that Fitzgerald does *not* reprise his Odyssey vessel-move here; he
addresses the goddess as a separate being. Mitchell is the one who picks that
thread back up — "sing through me."

### The wrath-word and its adjective (*oulomenên*)

| Translator | Noun | Adjective |
|---|---|---|
| Fitzgerald | "Anger" | "doomed and ruinous" |
| Fagles | "Rage" | "murderous, doomed" |
| Lombardo | "Rage" | "black and murderous" |
| Wilson | "wrath" | "cataclysmic" |
| Lattimore | "anger" | recast as a noun — "its devastation" |
| Pope | "wrath" | "direful" |

### Character epithets

In the Greek these are not context-sensitive. Achilles is "swift-footed" even
sitting still, because the epithet is part of his name.

| Formula | Renderings |
|---|---|
| *podas ôkus Achilleus* | Fitzgerald "swift-footed Akhilleus"; Lattimore keeps it rigidly every time; Fagles deliberately thins it out so it doesn't clang; Lombardo compresses |
| *korythaiolos Hektôr* | Fitzgerald and Lattimore "Hektor of the shining helm"; Fagles "Hector helmet flashing"; Wilson "Hector of the flashing helmet" |
| *nephelêgereta Zeus* | Fitzgerald "Zeus who gathers cloud"; Fagles "Zeus who marshals the storm clouds"; Lattimore "Zeus of the gathering clouds"; Wilson "cloud-gathering Zeus" |
| *épea pteróenta* | "winged words" — carries over from the Odyssey, kept by nearly all |

Hector's flashing helmet pays off later, when it frightens his own infant son.

### The carrion motif

*helôria teuche kynessin oiônoisi te pasi* — line 4 of the poem. Bodies left
unburied for dogs and birds.

| Translator | Rendering |
|---|---|
| Fitzgerald | "leaving so many dead men — carrion / for dogs and birds" |
| Fagles | "made their bodies carrion, / feasts for the dogs and birds" |
| Lattimore | "gave their bodies to be the delicate feasting / of dogs, of all birds" |
| Pope | "Whose limbs, unburied on the naked shore, / Devouring dogs and hungry vultures tore" |

Fitzgerald isolates "carrion" at a line-break. Fagles chooses "feasts," which
imports pleasure — the dogs are enjoying it. Lattimore's "delicate" looks like
a misfire and isn't; the Greek carries a ceremonial dreadfulness and he refuses
to smooth it. Pope adds the shore and the vultures — detail Homer only implies.

This motif has no counterpart in the Odyssey. The Odyssey repeats the sunrise
and the sea, cyclical and consoling. The Iliad repeats the unburied body, and
bookends on it: it opens on corpses left for dogs and closes on Priam begging
for Hector's body so it won't suffer that fate.

## Accuracy flags

These came out of a chat session and have not been checked against physical
copies. Most of the marquee lines are solid — Fitzgerald's dawn and opening,
Fagles's "man of twists and turns" and "Rage—", Wilson's "a complicated man,"
Lattimore's "man of many ways," Pope's couplet, and all the Butler (which is
public domain and easy to verify online). Treat the following as approximate
until spot-checked:

- Wilson's Odyssey dawn variants and her Athena renderings — she varies
  deliberately, so any single quoted form is one instance, not "her version."
- Green's *polytropos* line.
- Fitzgerald's Nestor, Menelaus, and Poseidon epithets.
- Fitzgerald's ship epithets ("beaked ships" / "long ships").
- Lattimore's exact swift-footed Achilles formula.

Also worth remembering when using Fitzgerald: he varied his epithets on
purpose, thinking mechanical repetition read as wooden in English. So a table
row implying "this is *the* Fitzgerald rendering" is slightly at odds with how
he actually worked.

## Using this at the table

Don't quote the modern translations into player-facing handouts — write our own
formulas in the same shape. The technique is what transfers:

1. **Fix the phrase and never vary it.** The power is in the sameness. The same
   words for the same moment, every session, until the players start saying it
   with you.
2. **Attach epithets to names, not to moments.** A captain is "the red-handed
   captain" while asleep, at dinner, in chains. That's what marks it as an
   inherited formula rather than description.
3. **Verb the adjective.** Fitzgerald's move — "spread out her fingertips of
   rose" rather than "rosy-fingered." Makes a static image act.
4. **One recurring image per body of material**, chosen to bookend. Homer picks
   the sunrise for one epic and the unburied corpse for the other.

Candidate hooks in existing material: dawn and tide formulas for the
[floating fleet](../lore/floating-fleet.md); a burial-and-unburied formula for
the [lineages and afterlives](../lore/lineages-and-afterlives.md) material,
where it would carry real mechanical weight.
