# Narration Lines — Read-Aloud by Trigger

*Real-world quotes collected for use at the table (not in-world canon, not
handout material). Grouped by when to say them: a time of day passing, a scene
opening, an event landing. Read them straight, without preamble — the line does
the work.*

**Related:** [Homeric epithets](homeric-epithets.md) for the technique of a
*fixed* recurring formula. These are the opposite tool — one-off lines, each
used once. If a line here starts getting used every session, move it to the
formula file and lock the wording.

---

## Dawn

> "The night is far spent, and the day is at hand."
> — Romans 13:12, KJV

> "And see — not day is it? No! Yet dawn creeps up the eastern slope."
> — Richard Wagner, *Siegfried* (tr. Jameson)

> "Morn in the white wake of the morning star came furrowing all the orient
> into gold."
> — Alfred, Lord Tennyson, *The Princess*

> "The morning had dawned clear and cold, with a crispness that hinted at the
> end of summer."
> — George R.R. Martin, *A Game of Thrones*

> "The light of morning, like a fresh thought, entered at the open windows."
> — Charles Dickens, *The Cricket on the Hearth*

## Midday

> "High noon behind the tamarisks — the sun is hot above us — as the wind is
> hot above us till the eye-strings break and ache."
> — Rudyard Kipling, *The Long Trail*

> "The hours passed, and the sun climbed to its noon height, when the shadows
> are shortest and the heat most fierce."
> — Homer, *Iliad* — see attribution flags below

## Dusk

> "It was the hour when the god of day was sinking below the western horizon,
> when the shadows were stretching long and purple across the plain."
> — Homer, *Iliad* — see attribution flags below

> "The sun, which had been beating fiercely on the plain all day, declined to
> the horizon, leaving a broad fan of crimson and gold."
> — Leo Tolstoy, *War and Peace* (tr. Pevear & Volokhonsky)

> "Slow sinks, more lovely ere his race be run, along Morea's hills the setting
> sun."
> — Lord Byron, *The Corsair*

> "Day, like a tired woman, folded her grey robes about her and lay down behind
> the far hills."
> — Willa Cather, *O Pioneers!* — see attribution flags below

> "The last light of evening gilded the battlements and crept down the stones
> to where they stood."
> — J.R.R. Tolkien, *The Fellowship of the Ring* — see attribution flags below

## Night

> "Already the light had died from the sky, and the darkness was creeping over
> the face of the waters."
> — Jules Verne, *Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea*

> "Night, the mother of fear and mystery, was coming upon me."
> — H.G. Wells, *The Time Machine*

> "The stars, that nature hung in heaven, and filled their lamps with
> everlasting oil, give due light to the misled and lonely traveller."
> — John Milton, *Comus*

> "The sky grew darker, painted blue on blue, one stroke at a time, into deeper
> and deeper shades of night."
> — Haruki Murakami, *Dance Dance Dance*

> "At midnight the siege and assault ceased, as if the enemy rested also."
> — John Bunyan, *The Holy War* — see attribution flags below

## Passage of time

Spanning the whole cycle — for a montage, a long voyage, a time skip.

> "The sun also ariseth, and the sun goeth down, and hasteth to his place where
> he arose."
> — Ecclesiastes 1:5, KJV

> "But now my task is smoothly done; I can fly, or I can run quickly to the
> green earth's end, where the bowed welkin slow doth bend."
> — John Milton, *Comus*

> "Time present and time past are both perhaps present in time future, and time
> future contained in time past."
> — T.S. Eliot, *Burnt Norton*

## Landfall and the sea

Split by the moment, since "landfall" covers four different beats. All of these
are public domain, so they can also go into handouts verbatim if a line earns
it.

### Land first sighted

> "They that go down to the sea in ships, that do business in great waters;
> these see the works of the LORD, and his wonders in the deep."
> — Psalm 107:23–24, KJV

> "We were the first that ever burst into that silent sea."
> — Samuel Taylor Coleridge, *The Rime of the Ancient Mariner*

> "There lies the port; the vessel puffs her sail: there gloom the dark, broad
> seas."
> — Alfred, Lord Tennyson, *Ulysses*

The Tennyson doubles as a callback — [Ulysses](../handouts/ulysses.md) is
already a handout, so the players may recognize the line, which is worth
something on a second landfall rather than a first.

### Coming ashore

> "Meditation and water are wedded for ever."
> — Herman Melville, *Moby-Dick*

> "I must down to the seas again, to the lonely sea and the sky."
> — John Masefield, *Sea-Fever*

Masefield is for a crew glad to be moving again; Melville for a landing that
wants stillness.

### An empty or ill-omened coast

> "Roll on, thou deep and dark blue Ocean — roll! Ten thousand fleets sweep
> over thee in vain."
> — Lord Byron, *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage*

> "Water, water, every where, nor any drop to drink."
> — Samuel Taylor Coleridge, *The Rime of the Ancient Mariner*

The Byron is the one for a shore that has swallowed fleets before — it says the
sea does not care, which is the note the
[drying of the valley](../lore/the-drying-of-the-valley.md) material keeps
reaching for.

### Putting out to sea

> "May I for my own self song's truth reckon, journey's jargon."
> — *The Seafarer* (tr. Ezra Pound)

Pairs with the existing [Seafarer handout](../handouts/seafarer-grave-gold.md).
Use one at departure and the other at the grave, and the players will connect
them without being told.

## Attribution flags

This set arrived from a chat session, and several attributions look wrong.
Worth checking before any of these gets read aloud as "this is Tolkien":

- **Both "Homer" lines (midday, dusk).** The midday one is labelled a
  paraphrase in the source, so it isn't a quote at all. The dusk one is
  credited to Pope — but Pope translated the *Iliad* in rhymed heroic couplets,
  and that line is prose. Almost certainly not his. Good lines; bad labels. Use
  them as our own narration, unattributed.
- **Cather, *O Pioneers!*** — "Day, like a tired woman" doesn't turn up in
  memory as hers. Unverified.
- **Tolkien, *Fellowship*** — "the last light of evening gilded the
  battlements" doesn't read as a line from the book. Unverified.
- **Bunyan, *The Holy War*** — unverified.

Solid as far as I can tell: the two KJV verses, Tennyson, Kipling, Byron, the
two Milton *Comus* passages, Dickens, Verne, Wells, Martin, Eliot. The Tolstoy
wording depends on which translation, and the Pevear & Volokhonsky credit is
worth confirming.

The safe move for any flagged line: keep the words, drop the attribution, and
treat it as ours. At the table nobody asks for a citation — the risk is only in
telling the players it's Tolkien when it isn't.

**On the landfall section:** those are my picks rather than yours, and I chose
only lines I'm confident are real and correctly attributed — which is why the
list is short. All are pre-1928 and public domain, so unlike the flagged set
above they're safe to reproduce verbatim in a player-facing handout. Still worth
a spot-check on the Pound *Seafarer* wording, which is the one I'd least want to
misquote.
