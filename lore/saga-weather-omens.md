# Saga weather-omens — real passages for the mage-wind foreshadowing

Verified public-domain saga lines to seed the two fleet engagements (see
[fleet-warfare](fleet-warfare.md)). The goal is to foreshadow the mage wind and
the fleet arrivals with **real saga text the party can be handed word-for-word**,
never invented lines (see the guardrail in [fleet-warfare](fleet-warfare.md) and
the sagas in [ships-and-sea-power](ships-and-sea-power.md)).

## Source and verification

All quotes below are from **Samuel Laing's translation of Snorri Sturluson's
*Heimskringla* (1844)** — public domain, safe to quote. Each was checked
character-by-character against the public-domain transcription at **Project
Gutenberg EBook #598** (https://www.gutenberg.org/files/598/598.txt). Cleaner
per-chapter renderings of the same 1844 text are on **Wikisource**
(`/wiki/Heimskringla/King_Olaf_Trygvason's_Saga/Part_I` and `/Part_III`) and
**sacred-texts** (`/neu/heim/`).

> **Before any of these becomes a player handout:** the Gutenberg-598 transcription
> carries scattered OCR typos; cross-check the exact apostrophes and spellings
> against the Wikisource page, which is a tidier rendering of the same text.
> Navigate the Svolder chapters by their **titles**, not the numbers — the chapter
> split differs between editions.

**That wording is not in Heimskringla.** Snorri/Laing's account of the

weigh a **pennyweight, not an ounce** (2A below). The vivid troll-women version —
Þorgerðr Hölgabrúðr and Irpa, arrows from every finger, ounce-hailstones — is from

Blake 1962, Finlay & Jóhannesdóttir 2018). Only the Old Norse (af Petersens ed.)

PD-verifiable quote. Options for the fix are in the chat / at the bottom here. The
Svolder quotes in that file should get the same verification pass — their wording
does not match Laing either.

## Image 1 — a wind that rises, turns, or is summoned (the mage wind)

**1A — Raud the Strong's wind from witchcraft.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch.
85 ("Thorer Hjort's Death").* The cleanest mage-wind line in the book: a wind
bought with sorcery.

> "Raud had always a fair wind wheresoever he wished to sail, which came from his
> arts of witchcraft; and, to make a short story, he came home to Godey."

**1B — Raud's contrary tempest, fair outside and a wall of storm within.** *King
Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch. 86 ("King Olaf's Voyage to Godey").* A worked wind that
turns against the attacker.

> "...he intended to sail into it to look for Raud, but a dreadful tempest and
> storm was raging in the fjord. They lay there a whole week, in which the same
> weather was raging within the fjord, while without there was a fine brisk wind
> only, fair for proceeding north along the land."

**1C — a fair wind everyone noticed.** *Magnus Erlingson's Saga, ch. 17 ("Of
Markus and King Sigurd").* Understated omen — a fleet the wind keeps favoring.

> "It was generally remarked, that Markus's men always got a fair wind, wherever
> they wished to sail to."

## Image 2 — weather called up by patrons (and the real Hjörungavágr storm)

**2A — the pennyweight hailstorm (the real Heimskringla storm at Hjörungavágr).**
*King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch. 44 ("Earl Sigvalde's Flight").*

> "A thunder-storm came on at this moment, and such a heavy hail-storm that every
> hailstone weighed a pennyweight."

**2B — Hakon sacrifices his son for the storm.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch.
47 ("Death of Gissur of Valders").* Together with 2A this is Heimskringla's
patron-summoned storm — a sacrifice, then the killing hail.

> "It was said that at this battle Earl Hakon had sacrificed for victory his son,
> young Erling, to the gods; and instantly came the hailstorm, and the defeat and
> slaughter of the Jomsborg vikings."

**2C — a sea-storm conjured against a fleet by witchcraft.** *Saga of Olaf
Haraldson (St. Olaf), ch. 8 ("The Third Battle").*

> "The Finlanders conjured up in the night, by their witchcraft, a dreadful storm
> and bad weather on the sea; but the king ordered the anchors to be weighed and
> sail hoisted, and beat off all night to the outside of the land. The king's luck
> prevailed more than the Finlanders' witchcraft..."

## Image 3 — fog concealing an approach

**3A — Eyvind Kelda's caps of darkness.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch. 70
("Eyvind Kelda's Death").* A shipload of sorcerers cloak their landing in a
conjured fog — then the magic fails them at the wrong moment. Fits the demons
coming ashore unseen in the fog.

> "Eyvind clothed them with caps of darkness, and so thick a mist that the king
> and his men could see nothing of them; but when they came near to the house at
> Ogvaldsnes, it became clear day."

## Image 4 — the dread of an approaching fleet

**4A — the kings on the headland at Svolder.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch.
111 ("Consultation of the Kings").* Grand ship after grand ship mistaken for the
flagship as the fleet files past.

> "...they saw among them one large and glancing ship. The two kings said, 'That
> is a large and very beautiful vessel: that will be the Long Serpent.' Earl Eirik
> replied, 'That is not the Long Serpent.'"

and the payoff a few lines on:

> "While the chiefs were speaking among themselves as above related, they saw
> three very large ships coming sailing along, and at last after them a fourth, and
> that was the Long Serpent."

**4B — the watchman's warning.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch. 110.*

> "...a skiff rowed out to inform the earl that the Danish king's army was lying in
> the harbour before them."

## Image 5 — death before surrender at sea / boarding

**5A — Olaf Tryggvason's death-leap.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch. 121 ("The
Serpent's Decks Cleared").*

> "King Olaf and Kolbjorn the marshal both sprang overboard, each on his own side
> of the ship; but the earl's men had laid out boats around the Serpent, and killed
> those who leaped overboard. Now when the king had sprung overboard, they tried to
> seize him with their hands, and bring him to Earl Eirik; but King Olaf threw his
> shield over his head, and sank beneath the waters."

and the skald Halfred's closing verse:

> "The Serpent and the Crane / Lay wrecks upon the main. / On his sword he cast a
> glance,— / With it he saw no chance. / To his marshal, who of yore / Many a
> war-chance had come o'er, / He spoke a word—then drew in breath, / And sprang to
> his deep-sea death."

**5B — Bue the Thick goes over with his gold.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch.
45 ("Bue Throws Himself Overboard").* (Gutenberg-598 prints `Bue s men` — a dropped
apostrophe; read *Bue's*.)

> "Then Bue lifted up two chests full of gold, and called aloud, 'Overboard all
> Bue['s] men,' and threw himself overboard with his two chests. Many of his people
> sprang overboard with him."

**5C — the Jomsviking executions.** *King Olaf Trygvason's Saga, ch. 46 ("Vikings
Bound Together in One Chain").* Heimskringla's PD version of the scene the
copyrighted Jómsvíkinga translations tell at more length.

> "One of them said, 'I will stick this cloak-pin that I have in my hand into the
> earth, if it be so that I know anything, after my head is cut off.' His head was
> cut off, but the cloak-pin fell from his hand."

> "There sat also a very handsome man with long hair, who twisted his hair over his
> head, put out his neck, and said, 'Don't make my hair bloody.' A man took the hair
> in his hands and held it fast. Thorkel hewed with his axe; but the viking twitched
> his head so strongly that he who was holding his hair fell forwards, and the axe
> cut off both his hands..."

## Image 6 — a storm wrecking a fleet on the voyage

**6A — Thiodolf's storm-verse.** *Saga of Magnus the Good, ch. 1.*

> "When thy brave ship left the land, / The bending yard could scarce withstand /
> The fury of the whistling gale, / That split thy many-coloured sail; / And many a
> stout ship, tempest-tost, / Was in that howling storm lost / That brought them
> safe to Sigtuna's shore, / Far from the sound of ocean's roar."

**6B — the Konungahella merchant fleet lost.** *Saga of Magnus the Blind and of
Harald Gille, ch. 10 ("The Rise of War in Konungahella").*

> "Thirteen loaded merchant ships made ready to leave the town, intending to
> proceed to Bergen; but eleven of them were lost, men and goods, and all that was
> in them; the twelfth was lost also, but the people were saved, although the cargo
> went to the bottom."

## How these seed the two engagements

- **The mage wind** ← Raud's wind from witchcraft (1A) is the real-world anchor;
  1B/1C give the turned wind and the noticed omen. Put the resonance in our own
  framing, or hand the party the Raud lines as a found saga-record.
- **The Dragonborn night landing** ← Eyvind's conjured fog (3A) for the demons in
  unseen; 2A–2C for weather called by patrons and by witchcraft; 4A for the dread
  of the fleet filing past.
- **The Floating Fleet's ship-to-ship** ← 4A/4B for the sighting and the warning;
  6A/6B for weather breaking a fleet; 5A–5C for the boarding once the fast craft
  close.
