# The Amseh Dynasty
## DM Reference — Full Spoilers

---

# OVERVIEW

The Amseh dynasty ruled the empire that once occupied the land now known as Maalm. Four kings across approximately 590 years, from agricultural prosperity through golden age through catastrophic collapse. The empire was destroyed by a combination of volcanic climate collapse (the Krakatoa-analog event, 5,000 years ago), military invasion, famine, political instability, and finally the desperate act of a foreign king sacrificing his children to gods who did not answer.

The tombs in the Valley of the Kings are what remains. The gods are dead. The empire is buried under Maalm. Ammit devoured the survivors.

The comet that triggered the Krakatoa eruption operates on a 1,000-year cycle. There have been three subsequent events. Those are TBD.

---

# ABSOLUTE CHRONOLOGY

Anchored to the **Bleeding Star** — the Krakatoa-analog eruption of Amseh III's Year 100 — at **~5,000 years ago**, the firm date the colossal statues and the comet cycle both fix. Working out from it with the reign lengths below:

| King | Role | Reign | Years ago |
|---|---|---|---|
| Amseh I | The Founder | 250 yr | ~5,550 – 5,300 |
| Amseh II | The Golden Age | 200 yr | ~5,300 – 5,100 |
| Amseh III | The Warrior King | 120 yr | ~5,100 – 4,980 (Bleeding Star ~5,000, his Year 100) |
| Amseh IV | The Last King | 20 yr | ~4,980 – 4,960 |

The empire fell and Ammit rose **~4,960 years ago**. The Dark Age that followed ran the better part of a thousand years, until the **founding of Maalm just over four thousand years ago (~4,100 ya)** — set deliberately in the trough *before* the comet's ~4,000-years-ago return began raising new land from the sea, which is why Beowulf found no other Dragonborn to rescue. (See [the world-history timeline](world-history-timeline.md).)

# THE EMPIRE

**Name:** [PLACEHOLDER — TBD]

**Location:** The land now occupied by Maalm and its surroundings.

**Religion:** The Egyptian-inspired pantheon — Osiris, Ra, Anubis, Thoth, Ma'at, and the full divine hierarchy. The gods were considered active and present in the world. Their silence during the final collapse was therefore not merely a theological crisis but an existential one — the entire structure of reality as the empire understood it failed simultaneously with its agriculture and military.

**Duration:** Approximately 590 years across four reigns, plus earlier kings who preceded the dynasty but lacked the resources for elaborate tombs.

# THE VALLEY OF THE KINGS

The valley predates the Amseh dynasty. The two colossal statues at its entrance — Amseh II with a serpent lower half, Amseh III in pharaoh form — were commissioned by Amseh III to honor his father and legitimize the dynasty. The statues were carved from the living rock of the canyon walls and stand approximately sixty feet tall.

The serpent-king figure (Amseh II) reflects his connection to the snake island and the trade relationship that defined his reign. The pharaoh figure (Amseh III) reflects his role as warrior-king and defender of the empire.

Each tomb contains crop yield and trade records for every year of that king's reign, inscribed in the walls alongside the religious imagery. These records are readable with a DC 12 Intelligence check or automatically for anyone who can read Ancient Egyptian. They are the most detailed economic history of the empire that survives.

# AMSEH I
## The Founder — 250 Year Reign

**Tomb:** Tomb 1 — Khepri, the tomb of transformation

**Race:** Elf — his 250-year reign was possible because of his elven lifespan.

**Historical role:** There were kings before Amseh I, but none wealthy enough to build elaborate tombs or leave lasting records. He is considered the dynasty's founder because he was the first king whose reign generated sufficient wealth to establish the monumental building program that the valley represents. His son's discovery of the snake island during his reign transformed the empire from a regional power into something larger.

**The Snake Island Discovery:**
During Amseh I's reign, his son — the future Amseh II — established first contact and then trade with the snake island depicted in the upper right corner of the ancient map. The island was home to a great serpent (the Serpent of Punt from the Shipwrecked Sailor tale) and a community of snake beings who had developed sophisticated agricultural technology over centuries.

The trade relationship brought two things to the empire:
- **Guano fertilizer** — the snake island maintained vast chicken farms to feed its serpent population. The surplus guano, traded to the empire, dramatically increased agricultural yields.
- **Permaculture methods and iron plow technology** — agricultural knowledge that compounded the guano's effects over time.

**Economic impact recorded in Tomb 1:**
The crop yield records show a 50% increase in agricultural output and a doubling of trade volume coinciding with the establishment of the snake island trade route. The records are specific — quantities of guano imported, fields treated, yields per region. Players who study them carefully will understand exactly what caused the prosperity.

**The ancient map:**
The inner chamber of Tomb 1 contains the ancient map showing the world as it was before the volcanic catastrophe — coastlines different, three islands absent that exist today, the snake island clearly marked in the upper right with a carefully rendered gold-and-lapis serpent. The map predates the catastrophe by centuries and reflects the empire at the height of its geographic knowledge.

**What the tomb tells us:**
Amseh I's tomb is about beginnings and preparation — the Opening of the Mouth ceremony, the Ba rising from the body, the canopic jars. It is the tomb of a man who built something and knew he was building it. The records of agricultural prosperity are his legacy statement.

# AMSEH II
## The Golden Age — 200 Year Reign

**Tomb:** Tomb 2 — Ra, the tomb of the sun

**The colossal statue:** The serpent-king figure at the valley entrance — human torso and arms, massive snake tail — represents Amseh II. Commissioned by his son Amseh III.

**Historical role:** Amseh II's reign was the dynasty's golden age. The snake island trade relationship his father established matured into a full cultural and commercial exchange. Crop yields and trade volumes from his reign show sustained prosperity — no dramatic increase beyond what Amseh I established, but consistent abundance across 200 years.

**The snake island relationship:**
Amseh II personally visited the snake island, according to the Shipwrecked Sailor tale preserved in his tomb's inner chamber. The tale describes a shipwreck, survival on the island, and an encounter with the great serpent — presented as the island's ruler and a benevolent figure who sent the sailor home with gifts. Whether this represents an actual voyage by Amseh II himself or a mythologized account of the trade relationship is unclear. The tale was important enough to be preserved in his inner burial chamber, which suggests it was personally significant.

The gifts described in the tale — perfumes, incense, ivory, apes, baboons, precious things — reflect actual trade goods from the snake island period.

**The serpent-king statue:**
Amseh II's lower half is depicted as a serpent — reflecting his connection to the snake island, the source of the dynasty's wealth. This was not considered monstrous in the empire's cultural context. The serpent was a symbol of prosperity, wisdom, and the divine knowledge that the snake island represented.

**Economic records in Tomb 2:**
Consistent with late Amseh I period — high agricultural yields, strong trade volumes, stable empire. The records show no crisis, no collapse, no warning of what was coming.

**What the tomb tells us:**
Amseh II's tomb is about the journey — the river of stars, the solar barque, the twelve hours of the night. It is the tomb of a king who understood the world as a cycle of departure and return, death and rebirth. The Shipwrecked Sailor story in his inner chamber is fundamentally about surviving catastrophe and coming home — which reads differently knowing what happened to his grandson.

# AMSEH III
## The Warrior King — 120 Year Reign

**Tomb:** Tomb 3 — Hapi, the tomb of the waters

**The colossal statue:** The pharaoh figure at the valley entrance — idealized, severe, arm extended in dominion — represents Amseh III. He commissioned both statues to honor his father and legitimize his own reign.

**Historical role:** Amseh III ruled for 120 years. The first century of his reign continued the prosperity of the golden age. In year 100, still in his prime, the world changed.

**The Bleeding Star (Year 100):**
A comet struck, triggering a massive volcanic eruption — the Krakatoa-analog event that reshaped the coastline, eliminated three islands, and caused a global climate collapse. The comet operates on a 1,000-year cycle. This was not the first event and would not be the last.

The immediate effects:
- Ash clouds blocking sunlight, destroying harvests
- Temperature drop causing widespread crop failure
- The snake island trade route disrupted — the island itself may have been affected or destroyed
- Famine beginning to spread across the empire

**The Sea Peoples Invasion (Year 100, concurrent):**
In the same year as the volcanic event, the Sea Peoples invaded — displaced peoples from across the known world, themselves driven by the climate catastrophe affecting their own lands. They came on rafts of skeletons, according to the tomb inscription, in the wake of the bleeding star.

Amseh III defeated the initial invasion — the Battle of Dijay, preserved in his tomb's inner chamber. His victory was genuine and significant. He trapped the invaders like wild fowl, as the inscription records, using both naval and land forces.

But the victory was pyrrhic. The climate collapse continued regardless of military success. Subsequent battles went less well. The famine deepened. The snake island trade — the foundation of two centuries of prosperity — collapsed entirely.

**The coup:**
As the situation deteriorated, the palace moved against Amseh III. He was removed from power — the exact circumstances are not recorded in the tombs, but the result is clear. His older children, who would have been his successors, were killed in the coup. Amseh III himself was killed or died shortly after being deposed. He was buried in his tomb while the empire was already failing.

**Economic records in Tomb 3:**
The crop yield records in Tomb 3 are the most dramatic in the valley. A century of sustained prosperity followed by a cliff-edge collapse in year 100 — yields dropping to a fraction of their previous levels, trade records showing the snake island route going silent, military expenditure records appearing for the first time. Players who compare Tomb 3's records to Tombs 1 and 2 will understand exactly when and how fast the empire fell.

**What the tomb tells us:**
Amseh III's tomb is about battle and judgment simultaneously. The outer chamber shows his military victory. The inner chamber shows three divine judges — Neha-hau, Am-khaibitu, Hept-Shet — assessing a soul. Thoth records the verdict. The juxtaposition is intentional: a king who won his war and lost his empire, waiting to be judged for both.

# AMSEH IV
## The Last King — 20 Year Reign

**Tomb:** Tomb 4 — Osiris, the tomb of judgment

**Historical role:** Amseh IV was not born to be king. He was a younger prince, married off to a foreign court as part of a diplomatic arrangement — the Mesopotamian theme of his tomb reflects his foreign origin and the cultural world he came from before his return. When the coup killed his older siblings, he was retrieved from abroad and placed on the throne.

He changed his name to Amseh to legitimize his claim. His original name is unrecorded in the tombs — either deliberately erased or simply never inscribed, as if he was trying to become someone else entirely.

**The foreign queen:**
He brought Kethara (Farseh-ti) with him from the foreign court — she was his wife from his life abroad. She was foreign in a court already under enormous stress, ruling a people who were starving and looking for someone to blame. There is evidence — in the tone of the lament inscription if not in explicit text — that the court pushed for the sacrifice of the children partly out of xenophobia. The foreign queen's children. The children who were not fully of this land. The gods might prefer pure-blooded offerings.

The gods said nothing either way.

**The seven sacrifices:**
Amseh IV sacrificed his seven children over the course of his twenty-year reign as the situation grew increasingly desperate. Not all at once — one by one, as each previous sacrifice failed to produce divine response. The seven small coffins in his unfinished inner chamber are painted in blue — the best pigment available, more carefully rendered than anything else in the tomb. Someone used what they had left for those.

The gods remained silent throughout.

**The collapse:**
The city collapsed. The people starved. Ammit rose from the ruins — gorged and maddened by a thousand years of condemned dead and the uneaten hearts of ten thousand innocent children — and devoured the survivors. The dark age began.

**The unfinished tomb:**
Tomb 4 was never completed. The painters stopped mid-work — scaffolding marks in the stone, red ochre outlines that were never filled in, a section of ceiling with plaster applied but nothing drawn. The central composition was finished because someone made sure of that. The seven blue coffins were placed carefully. But the rest was abandoned before it could be completed.

Whether Amseh IV died before the tomb was finished, or whether the empire simply ran out of people capable of finishing it, is not recorded.

**Kethara/Farseh-ti:**
She outlived him. She remained in the valley — unable or unwilling to leave — and eventually became what she is now: an ancient mummy who eases the suffering of the lepers who shelter in Tomb 1. Her motivation is legible in the valley itself: this is where her children are buried. She could not save them. She can at least stay.

Her name is inscribed beside Amseh IV's cartouche in the inner chamber — not above it, not below it, but beside it, as an equal. Someone took time with that inscription that they didn't have.

**Economic records in Tomb 4:**
There are no meaningful trade or crop records in Tomb 4. The reign was too short and too catastrophic to produce the kind of sustained data the earlier tombs contain. What records exist show only collapse — yields near zero, trade nonexistent, military expenditure replaced by ritual expenditure as sacrifice became the empire's primary response to crisis.

**What the tomb tells us:**
Amseh IV's tomb is the only unfinished one in the valley. It is the most elaborate in its puzzle room — the full Hall of Two Truths, all the gods named, Ammit waiting — and the most broken in its inner chamber. A kneeling king with his crown slightly askew, reaching toward empty thrones. Seven blue coffins. A name carved beside his as an equal. The tomb of a man who did everything wrong for reasons that were not entirely his fault, in a situation that was not entirely of his making, and who went to his judgment anyway.

# THE SERPENT-KING STATUES — FULL CONTEXT

The two colossal figures at the valley entrance:

**Left figure — Amseh II:**
Human torso and face, serpent lower half. The serpent represents his connection to the snake island and the source of the dynasty's wealth. One hand rests on his chest, one extends outward in a gesture of regal judgment. Commissioned posthumously by his son.

**Right figure — Amseh III:**
Full pharaoh form, idealized and severe. The uraeus serpent diadem at his brow. One arm extended forward, open-handed, claiming dominion over the valley. Commissioned by himself, as part of a legitimization program following the coup that brought him to power.

Both figures display what might be called cold command — the expressions of rulers who have seen everything and found most of it wanting. They were carved while the empire was still powerful enough to execute work of this scale. By the time Amseh IV ruled, such a project would have been impossible.

The statues have been here for approximately 5,000 years. They are weathered but intact.

# THE SNAKE ISLAND — ADDITIONAL NOTES

The snake island is the tiny island in the upper right corner of the ancient map in Tomb 1 — marked with a gold-and-lapis serpent rendered in more detail than anything else on the map. Its current status is unknown. The three islands that were absent from the ancient map now exist — created by volcanic activity over the intervening millennia. Whether the snake island survived the Krakatoa-analog event is unclear.

The Serpent of Punt from Amseh II's tomb is the island's ruler as of his reign — a being thirty cubits long, body overlaid with gold, color like lapis lazuli. Whether this being still lives, whether the island still exists, and whether the trade relationship could theoretically be reestablished are open questions for the campaign.

The chicken farm / guano economy: the snake island maintained vast chicken farms to feed its serpent population. The surplus guano was the primary trade commodity exported to the empire — a fertilizer that, combined with permaculture methods and iron plow technology also acquired through the trade relationship, produced the dramatic agricultural yields recorded in Tombs 1 and 2. The chicken farms also explain the egg supply that sustained the serpent population.

# CAMPAIGN CONNECTIONS

**The comet cycle:**
The comet operates on a 1,000-year cycle. The Krakatoa-analog event was 5,000 years ago. There have been three subsequent events. The current campaign takes place in year [TBD] of the current cycle. Whether another event is coming — and when — is a question the campaign's volcanic and geological themes will eventually address.

**Ammit and Maalm:**
Ammit devoured the survivors of the empire's collapse and took up residence in the ruins. The better part of a thousand years later — just over four thousand years ago, in the trough before the comet's return began raising new land — Beowulf fought her and the city of Maalm was built above them both. The party walks on ground where Ammit — or what remains of her after Beowulf — sleeps. This has not been made explicit to them.

**The wizard and the names:**
The wizard sent the party to collect names from the tombs. The names of four kings, multiple gods, and Kethara/Farseh-ti are findable. Whether the wizard wants royal names specifically, divine names, or all names is something the Yochlol, Mul, has not clarified. The theological connection between Egyptian soul theory (the Ren as a component of the soul) and the Planar Binding mechanic suggests the wizard understands something about names that the party does not yet.

**The snake island as a future location:**
The ancient map shows where the snake island was. The party has the map. Whether the island still exists, what happened to the Serpent of Punt during and after the volcanic catastrophe, and whether contact could be reestablished are open campaign questions.

**Kethara/Farseh-ti as a living connection:**
Kethara is the only surviving member of the Amseh dynasty — the only person alive who knew Amseh IV personally, who was present for the sacrifices, who watched the empire collapse from inside the palace. She has been in this valley for 5,000 years. If the party ever finds a way to communicate with her meaningfully, she is the most complete historical source available. She has chosen not to speak.

**The xenophobia thread:**
The court pushed for the sacrifice of Amseh IV's children partly because they were the foreign queen's children — not fully of the land, not fully trusted. This theme — the persecution of those who exist between worlds, who are not fully of one place or another — connects to Aniess (transformed by the Order into something between human and snake), to the demon wife's children (between mortal and Abyssal), and to the broader campaign theme of binding beings who don't fit neatly into one category.
