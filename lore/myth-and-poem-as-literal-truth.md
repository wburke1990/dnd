# Myth & Poem as Literal Truth — a design pillar

> **The generative question:** *What if this were literally true?* Take a
> real myth, or a real poem, treat its strangest claim as plain fact about
> Nila, and follow it down until the world goes weird. This is the engine
> behind Maalm's Egypt, and it already runs on poetry too — see the dream
> quest below. Companion to [`campaign-overview.md`](campaign-overview.md)
> §3.D and §5.

---

## 1. Why

Most people carry a sanded-down version of their ancestors' myths — a few
gods, a moral or two. They do not grasp how strange the real material is:
Medjed the blind stalking blanket, the forty-two assessors who each eat a
different sin, a soul cut into seven parts that can die separately. The
campaign puts that strangeness back by running real primary-source myth
**unsanitized and literally true** — one cosmology per region, played
straight (see the region → myth table in
[`campaign-overview.md`](campaign-overview.md) §5).

The same trick works on poems. A poem states something impossible and asks
you to hold it for the length of the poem. This pillar holds it past the
last line. If three children really did sail a wooden shoe into a sea of
stars, then that sea is a real place, and closing your eyes is how you reach
it.

## 2. Myth as literal truth (established)

Already the spine of the world; see
[`campaign-overview.md`](campaign-overview.md) §3.D. In brief:

- **One region, one real mythology, played straight.** Egypt (Maalm) was
  built first — see [`egyptian-afterlife-mythology.md`](egyptian-afterlife-mythology.md),
  [`maalm-founding.md`](maalm-founding.md), [`bestiary/egypt.md`](../bestiary/egypt.md).
- **Literal, not decorative.** Ammit sleeps under Maalm. The Ren (true
  name) owns a soul with no save. The Weighing of the Heart is a thing that
  happens to the dead here. The myth is the physics.
- **Unsanitized.** Keep the primary-source weirdness — Medjed, the 42
  assessors, the seven-part soul, the Serpent of Punt — verbatim, sources
  cited (e.g. [`bestiary/akhekh.md`](../bestiary/akhekh.md) carries a
  real-world-sources section).

## 3. Poem as literal truth (the method)

Same question, aimed at a poem instead of a myth. Take the poem's central
impossible claim, declare it a fact of Nila, and work outward:

1. **State the literal claim.** Strip the metaphor. What does the poem
   assert if no one is speaking figuratively?
2. **Read every level of the poem at once.** A poem often means two things
   — a surface image and a hidden one (a voyage that is also a child falling
   asleep). Don't pick. Take *both* readings as simultaneously, literally
   true, and the fusion is where the strangeness lives (see the dream quest,
   §4).
3. **Follow the consequences.** If that is true, what else must be true?
   Who lives near it, who uses it, what does it do to the people it touches?
4. **Let it be its own thing.** A literalized poem does **not** have to
   serve the main plot or plug into the comet / name / transformation
   engine. It can just be a strange true fact of the world, standing on its
   own. (If it happens to connect — the dream quest does, hard — fine. That
   is not the goal, and forcing every wonder to be about the one thing
   flattens the world.)
5. **Keep the source weird.** Same rule as the myths: don't tidy the poem
   into sense. The leftover strangeness is the point.

## 4. Worked example — *Wynken, Blynken, and Nod* (already built)

Eugene Field, 1889. Handout (verbatim):
[`handouts/wynken-blynken-and-nod.md`](../handouts/wynken-blynken-and-nod.md).
This poem is **already realized in the campaign** as the incantation for the
**Dream of the Blue Veil** — see [`the-dream-quest.md`](../encounters/the-dream-quest.md).
It is the clearest example of the method, because it runs on **two levels at
once**:

- **The poem's surface (stanzas 1–3):** three fishermen sail a wooden shoe
  up a river of crystal light into a sea where the little stars are
  herring-fish, and cast nets of silver and gold to catch them.
- **The poem's reveal (stanza 4):** it is a **lullaby**. Wynken and Blynken
  are a sleeping child's two eyes, Nod is the head, the wooden shoe is the
  trundle-bed. The whole voyage is a child closing their eyes and going to
  sleep. *"So shut your eyes while Mother sings."*

**Literalized, both are true together:** sleep **is** the boat. Closing your
eyes launches the shoe. The sea of stars you reach that way is a real
place — the Duat, a black river with stars above and below where the boat
moves without wind (the dream quest's opening read-aloud is the poem's sea
of dew, played straight). The lullaby layer and the voyage layer are the
same event: the child's shut eyes and the launched shoe are one thing.

And the family layer falls out of that for free: **Ghassan read the poem to
his half-demon children every night as a lullaby, and it was always the
incantation.** He sang them the literal route to an afterlife they can never
enter, and no one in the house connected the bedtime rhyme to a real river
until the party woke inside it. That chill is the pillar working — a poem
taken at its word until it turns into a place.

## 5. How a literalized poem attaches to the map

**Resolved: poems are a flexible layer, not one fixed structure.** A poem
can anchor a whole location *or* seed a smaller wonder inside an existing
region — whichever the poem wants.

- **As a place.** The poem's world is big enough to sail to — its own pin on
  Preem's map, a destination beside the myth-regions.
- **As a seeded wonder.** The poem-truth lives *inside* a region as a pocket
  of strangeness — found, not sailed to. **The dream quest is this pattern
  already:** *Wynken, Blynken, and Nod* is seeded inside Maalm as one
  grieving family's private rite, discovered in a merchant's house, not
  marked on any map.

Pick per poem. Big cosmological claims tend to want a place; small domestic
ones tend to want a seed.

### Sketches for the two unbuilt ones

**Do Not Stand at My Grave and Weep** ([handout](../handouts/do-not-stand-at-my-grave.md)).
The dead speaks and says the grave does not hold her: *"I am not there; I did not
die."* Taken at its word, she is not somewhere else — she is the weather now. The
buried become the thousand winds, the glints on snow, the sunlight on the grain,
the autumn rain, the birds at dawn, the stars at night. Follow it down: the weather
of that place is made of its dead. Every wind is someone. The rain falls on the
fields and the grain grows in their light. To weep at a graveside is to stand in
the one place the person is not. A storm is many of them at once. Whether a wind
can hear you, or answer, is left open. **Placement:** wants a place or a whole
people's death-custom — a burial-ground you visit, or a region whose dead disperse
into the sky and the fields — rather than a seed.

**The Wanderer** ([handout](../handouts/the-wanderer-lament.md)). *"Where is the
horse gone? Where the rider? … How that time has passed away, dark under the cover
of night, as if it had never been."* Taken at its word, the questions are not
rhetorical and the last line is not a figure of speech: what passes out of glory
and memory is **unmade** — deleted backward, as if it had never stood. Renown holds
a thing in existence; the night takes what renown no longer holds up. Annibel, who
besieged Anklia and lost and can no longer say who she was, is going this way — her
cavalry and captains not only dead but ceasing ever to have been, and the silent
northern colonies going quiet and then simply not being there. **Placement:**
already seeded in Musleheim and on Annibel (see [the Muspel empire](the-muspel-empire.md));
a regional truth carried by the fallen war-host, not a place to sail to.

## 7. Guardrail

The failure mode is the same for both halves: sanding the source down until
it makes comfortable sense. Don't. A myth kept only for its aesthetic, or a
poem "explained," loses the thing this pillar exists to deliver — the felt
strangeness of a world our ancestors actually described and actually
believed. Give the players the weird fact, played straight, and let them
decide what to make of it.
