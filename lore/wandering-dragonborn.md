# The Wandering — Dragonborn Saints of the Sea

The seafaring dragonborn religion: a veneration of solitary wandering
heroes who took tiny boats out into Nila bay alone, did deeds the songs
still sing of, and died far from anyone. Where one of them fell, his
people raise a **watch-stone** — a standing stone or rough statue facing
the water, often over an empty grave — and passing wanderers keep the
watch a while before sailing on. Seeded by the 6/19/26 backstory chat
(the "mythological wandering dragonborn"); the **Bedraageon** grave under
[Maalm](maalm-founding.md) is the eldest instance, and the orphan
[Requiem](../handouts/requiem.md) grave now belongs to one of them.

> Legend: **[settled]** = grounded in chat · **[loose]** = floated, not
> locked · **[draft]** = invented here for the DM to keep, cut, or rename.

## The Phoenician forebears — the lost tribes

Where the wandering dragonborn *come from*. **`[DM ruling — Will, 6/26]`**
Today's solitary roamers are the descendants of the **Bronze-Age Phoenician
dragonborn**: the trade-and-colony seafarers of ~5,000 years ago, who dwindled
over the Ragnaroks since into the scattered lone wanderers of the present. (The
halfling **Floating Fleets** are *not* their descendants — a separate people;
see [floating-fleet.md](floating-fleet.md).)

**Dragonborn are the Phoenicians of Nila** — the trade-and-colony people, not a
war-state. Their mother-city was **Lonka, their Tyre**: at once the holy
first-perch of Taniyn (see [taniyn-and-lonka.md](taniyn-and-lonka.md)) *and* the
metropolis their colonists scattered from — until the **Bleeding Star**
obliterated it ~5,000 ya (it is now **Jotunheim**). Somewhere out among those
old colonies is a **ruined ancient dragonborn settlement** in the
Carthage/Tunisia mold — the conquerors salted the earth and massacred the
inhabitants, but **survivors cling on in hidden coves and cliffside caves**,
integrated with local peoples and half-forgotten. **Lost tribes of dragonborn,
scattered to the far corners** — a find-the-lost-tribes quest that rhymes with
both **Bedraageon's** fruitless search ([maalm-founding.md](maalm-founding.md)) and
the **children-of-Taniyn** cosmology. The **Muspel "missing fleet"** that sailed
**north toward the lost colonies** ([the Muspel empire §5](the-muspel-empire.md))
points straight at this seam. **[loose — offered, not locked]**

> **Two Punic threads, deliberately split.** The *trade-and-colony* Phoenicians
> are **here** — the dragonborn, Lonka-as-Tyre. The *Punic War* (Annibel/Hannibal, the
> crossings, a Zama) lives with the **orc Muspel war-state** instead, recast as
> Viking/barbarian herdsmen who fight their **own** wars (see
> [the Muspel empire §5](the-muspel-empire.md)). Carthage's mercantile,
> mercenary-hiring, Sicily-obsessed side carries over to neither.

## The shape of the faith

- **Real-world flavor:** the Maghrebi **marabout** — wandering Sufi
  holy-men and warrior-saints whose little domed tombs dot the North
  African landscape and become pilgrimage sites generations after they
  die. Map that onto the sea: the saints' graves dot the **islands**, and
  the sea-roads between them are a pilgrimage no one calls a pilgrimage.
  **[draft]**
- **The creed is a longing, not a doctrine.** What every wanderer shares
  is **the call** — the pull back out onto the water that "may not be
  denied." Masefield's **"Sea-Fever"** *is* that creed, recited the way
  scripture is recited; its phrases are the faith's working vocabulary.
  This is how "Sea-Fever" gets woven in — not as one quest, but as the
  **liturgy of the Wandering**. (Full text in
  [blackacre.md](../characters/blackacre.md).) **[draft — resolves an
  open thread]**
- **Death is "the long trick."** From Sea-Fever's last line — *"and quiet
  sleep and a sweet dream when the long trick's over."* To die is to
  **stand the long trick** / **keep the last watch**. A watch-stone is
  where a saint stands it forever, eyes to sea. Pilgrims who stop there
  "**keep the watch**" — sit the night, leave water and a coin, recite a
  verse, and go. **[draft]**
- **No temples, no clergy.** The graves are the whole church. There is no
  hierarchy — fitting for a people with no single ruler (see the
  [Floating Fleet](../characters/blackacre.md)'s chiefdom of many heads).
  Any wanderer can keep any watch.
- **The songs disagree on purpose.** These are **demigod tall-tales** —
  Heracles, Maui, Coyote, Cú Chulainn (the exact comparisons Ahms
  reached for). Every island tells its own version, contradictory and
  growing; arguing the "true" telling over drinks *is* the devotion.
  **[settled — per chat]**

## The Eldest — Bedraageon

The progenitor and the template: a dragonborn who crossed the water alone
(well — twelve companions, none of them his people), went down into the
dark to face the thing no one else could, and **died locked in the
embrace of his kill**. Buried in the sun where he fell; a city grew over
the rest. His flush grave-stone in Maalm's plaza and the mural in the
tavern called *The Twelve* are described in
[maalm-founding.md](maalm-founding.md). Every saint below is, in some
sense, a retelling of him. **[settled — existing canon]**

## The Roster of the Wandering

Four named saints, one per archetype Ahms named. Names are **[draft]** —
Berber/Arabic flavor, free to rename. Each gets feats the songs sing, the
island where the watch-stone stands, and a grave inscription (the firm
ones reuse poems already in the repo; the rest are flagged).

### Idris the Tide-Strong — *the Heracles*

- **The strong one, the monster-hunter.** Songs: he **wrestled the kraken
  that swallowed a whole tribe's fleet and pulled the crews back out
  through its gills, alive**; carried his holed boat across a drying reef
  on his back; **drank a whirlpool down** to walk the seabed and back.
- **The labors as penance.** The best-loved cycle: Idris wronged a chief
  (the tellings differ on how), and the chief set him **a chain of
  impossible labors as the price of return.** He died on the last one.
  *This penance-through-deeds shape is the one that gets under Blackacre's
  skin* — it's his own situation in legend.
- **Death & watch-stone:** old, hunting the one beast that had once
  bested him; found, like Bedraageon, **locked with it in the embrace of
  their ending.** Watch-stone on a windward headland (placement TBD —
  near Lonka works, to put it on Blackacre's road).
- **Inscription:** Stevenson's **"Requiem"** — *"Home is the sailor, home
  from sea, / And the hunter home from the hill."* The orphan grave at
  [requiem.md](../handouts/requiem.md) is **Idris's**. **[draft — but
  ready to commit; it adopts an existing handout]**

### Anzar the Island-Fisher — *the Maui*

- **The shaper of the world.** The myth that explains the map: the
  **volcanic islands of the central sea were not always there** (canon —
  pre-Fall the deep was open water; see
  [tomb-1-inner-chamber-ancient-map.md](../prompts/tomb-1-inner-chamber-ancient-map.md)).
  Anzar **fished them up from the deep on a line of his own sinew.**
- **Trickster-benefactor feats:** **snared the dawn** to slow a sunrise so
  a becalmed crew wouldn't bake; **stole the trade-winds** and corked them
  in a wineskin; **taught the halflings and dragonborn to read the
  stars** (the practical gift behind "a star to steer her by"). Named for
  **Anzar**, the real Amazigh water/rain deity — apt for the one who
  shaped the waters. **[draft]**
- **Death & watch-stone:** drowned **pulling up one island too many** —
  hubris, or the line simply snapped. No body was ever found, so his is a
  **cenotaph**: a lone basalt stack on the **youngest volcanic isle**,
  watch but no grave.
- **Inscription:** **[draft / TBD]** — a fragment about the sea taking
  back what it lends. Good slot for a player or DM choice later.

### Wazzan the Laughing — *the Coyote*

- **The trickster, foolish-clever, the pirates' favorite.** Songs:
  **cheated a sea-god at dice for a year of fair winds**; **stole fire
  from the zealots' great lighthouse** and carried it off in his mouth;
  **painted a second sail on a rock** and lured a kingdom's galley-fleet
  onto it in the dark. Half the songs he's the hero, half the butt — and
  he **lies differently in every one.** Spiritual cousin of Juha/Nasreddin
  the folk-trickster. **[draft]**
- **The joke is whether he's even dead.** Sailors still claim to see his
  patched sail on the horizon at dusk. **Three different islands each
  swear they hold his grave** — and at least one of those graves has been
  **robbed from the inside** (he stole his own grave-goods on the way
  out, the song says). Perfect comic relief against the solemn watches —
  and a real mystery hook if you want him alive somewhere.
- **Inscription:** **[draft / TBD]** — wherever it's carved, it should
  read like a punchline.

### Tariq the Last-Standing — *the Cú Chulainn*

- **The berserker, the warrior who would not give ground.** Songs: the
  **salt-rage** took him in battle (the warp-spasm — he came back wrong
  for a day after); he **burned his own boats on the beach** so his band
  could not flee, and they won; he **held a strait alone against a whole
  fleet** (single boat at the strait, the way Cú Chulainn held the ford).
  Named for **Tariq** (the Berber who burned the boats at Gibraltar —
  *Jabal Tariq*). **[draft]**
- **Death & watch-stone:** mortally wounded and refusing to fall, he
  **lashed himself upright to his own mast** (or to a standing stone) so
  he would **die on his feet, facing the enemy** — and the enemy did not
  dare come closer until a **gull lit on his shoulder** and they knew. The
  watch-stone on that cape is **shaped like a man at the tiller**, and is
  the one sailors salute passing the strait.
- **Inscription:** **[draft]** — Henley's **"Invictus"** fits to the bone
  (*"bloodied, but unbowed … I am the master of my fate"*); Tennyson's
  **"Ulysses"** closing (*"to strive, to seek, to find, and not to
  yield"*) is the alternate. Either would make a clean companion handout
  to [requiem.md](../handouts/requiem.md).

## Blackacre and the Wandering

The character arc Ahms named on 6/19: **judgy and unimpressed at first,
growing to associate with them.** How to run it:

- **At first he scoffs.** He's a practical pirate; the watch-stone cult
  reads to him as sentiment — *drowned show-offs with good publicists*,
  loners romanticized by people who never had to bail water. He'll keep
  the watch because it's rude not to, and roll his eyes doing it.
- **The myth is his own life in disguise.** He is *already* a wanderer who
  walked away from a great deed (the [Sayid](../characters/blackacre.md) ship-seizure he
  bailed on when it turned violent) and is groping toward how to atone.
  **Idris's penance-labors** are his exact shape; **Tariq's won't-give-
  ground** is the courage he bailed on. He doesn't notice the rhyme at
  first.
- **The conversion is geographic.** Because the graves are **scattered
  across the islands he has to sail through anyway**, he keeps literally
  arriving at them. Stumbling onto watch-stone after watch-stone — each
  song landing a little closer to home — is what turns the eye-roll into
  something he won't admit is faith. By the time it matters, **Sea-Fever
  is his actual creed**, and the saints are his model for what atonement
  might cost: finish the thing, even if finishing it is the long trick.
- **Foreshadowing:** this quietly sets up a heroic-sacrifice option
  for the Sayid/Lonka climax without forcing it — the faith gives dying
  to mend things a *meaning* Blackacre would once have laughed at.
- **Practice for the endgame choice.** `[draft]` At the finale the
  Jotunheim dragonborn ask Blackacre to **lead their fleet** to Moku, and
  what he does with it is the climax of his arc (see
  [blackacre.md](../characters/blackacre.md) and
  [lonka-jotunheim.md §6](lonka-jotunheim.md)). Use the watch-stones to put
  smaller moral choices in front of him first — the price of a deed, mercy
  or the sword, whether holy ground is worth blood. **Don't script the
  answers:** tune each one to where his character is at the time, and let
  him decide.

## Encounter hooks

1. **The watch-stone you can't avoid.** Drop a grave on the route to any
   sea destination. First few times: flavor, a verse, a free drink owed
   to the dead. Repetition is what converts
   him.
2. **Two tellings, one tavern.** Two NPCs argue the "true" version of an
   Idris or Wazzan song; pull Blackacre in. Cheap, in-character, and it
   teaches the players the canon painlessly.
3. **A living saint?** Play the Wazzan-still-alive rumor straight once —
   a patched sail that shouldn't be there — and let them decide if the
   trickster cheated death too.
4. **Idris's last labor unfinished.** Frame a quest as **completing a
   labor the saint died before finishing**; let Blackacre be the one who
   closes the cycle. Maximum resonance with his atonement arc.
5. **Tariq's strait.** Stage a hold-the-line set-piece at the cape with
   the tiller-shaped stone — the place where the faith says you do not
   give ground.
