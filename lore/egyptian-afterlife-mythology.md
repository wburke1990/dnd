# Egyptian Afterlife Mythology
## DM Reference — Full Spoilers

---

# OVERVIEW

The Egyptian afterlife belief system evolved over approximately 3,000 years and changed dramatically between periods. The dream quest draws primarily from New Kingdom sources — the Book of the Dead, the Amduat, the Book of Gates — but the tombs in the Valley of the Kings reference all three major periods. Understanding the evolution helps you answer player questions consistently and adds depth to how you describe what the party encounters.

The single most important thing to understand: Egyptian afterlife belief was not a fixed system. It changed constantly, it contradicted itself, and different texts reflect different theological schools sometimes operating simultaneously. This is a feature, not a bug — it means you have enormous flexibility in how you present things at the table.

# THE THREE MAJOR PERIODS

## Old Kingdom (c. 2686–2181 BCE) — The Pyramid Texts

**Who had access:** The pharaoh only. Later, royal consorts.

**The destination:** The sky — specifically the circumpolar stars that never set, called the imperishable stars. The afterlife was celestial, not subterranean.

**The journey:** The pharaoh's soul ascended to the heavens rather than descending into an underworld. Three theologies existed simultaneously and somewhat uneasily:

- **Stellar theology** — the pharaoh becomes a star, joins the gods in the sky
- **Solar theology** — the pharaoh merges with Ra, the sun god
- **Osirian theology** — the pharaoh becomes Osiris, travels through the underworld

**Key phases of the stellar ascent:**

1. Claiming earthly authority — gods from all corners of Egypt acknowledge the pharaoh's supremacy
2. Departure from the body — spells free the soul and launch it skyward
3. The sky crossing — the soul flies, climbs a ladder, or takes a celestial ferry
4. Navigating the ferryman — if the boatman refuses, the king threatens or outmaneuvers him
5. The Cannibal Hymn — the pharaoh hunts and consumes the gods to absorb their divine power. This is metaphorical: by consuming the gods he becomes divine, capable of completing the ascent. The hymn is disturbing but understood as a ritual of absorption rather than literal cannibalism.
6. Acceptance and union — the pharaoh is received among the gods and the imperishable stars

**The texts:** 760 spells inscribed on pyramid walls. No single pyramid contains all of them. They were oral tradition before being written down — some spells reference funerary practices that predate the pyramids themselves.

**What this means for your campaign:** The Amseh kings predate the New Kingdom theology that the dream quest uses. Their tombs reference the older stellar and Osirian traditions. The empty thrones in Tomb 4 — gods who do not answer — reflect the collapse of the divine order that sustained the Old Kingdom cosmology.

## Middle Kingdom (c. 2055–1650 BCE) — The Coffin Texts

**Who had access:** Non-royal elites who could afford decorated coffins. The first democratization of the afterlife.

**The destination:** The Duat — the underworld, ruled by Osiris. The afterlife shifted from sky to subterranean.

**Key changes from the Old Kingdom:**

- The soul descends rather than ascends
- Osiris becomes central rather than peripheral
- Maps of the underworld appear for the first time — the earliest known maps from any culture
- The Book of Two Ways introduces navigational choice: a land route and a water route through the Duat, separated by a Lake of Fire

**The Book of Two Ways:**

The most important Middle Kingdom innovation for your campaign. Two paths through the underworld:

- **Land route (black):** Darker, more dangerous, guarded by named demons at a series of gates
- **Water route (blue):** Requires navigating fire beneath the surface

Both routes lead to Osiris and the Field of Offerings. Both routes also contain dead ends, demons, and circles of fire. The text contains the spells needed to get past the guardians of the gates.

**Seven gates with three guardians each.** The first gate is guarded by "the One who Rejects the Ignorant" — those without knowledge cannot pass. Beyond it: two alternatives, toward light or toward darkness.

Guardian names include: "the sad-voiced one," "he whose face is covered," "dog face," "scowler," "he whose face is dreadful," "monster," "she of the knife," "Hippopotamus face, bellowing of power," "He who lives on Maggots," "He who eats the droppings of his hinder parts."

At the end of the journey: Osiris offers the Eye of Horus. The soul becomes one with Thoth and spends eternity in the presence of Ra.

**What this means for your campaign:** The Book of Two Ways is the direct ancestor of the gate structure in the dream quest. Gate Five — Isis, Nephthys, and Selket above a locked gate on the river — echoes this tradition. The Lake of Fire appears as blue fire in Gate Six.

## New Kingdom (c. 1550–1070 BCE) — The Book of the Dead, Amduat, and Book of Gates

**Who had access:** Any Egyptian who could afford their own copy. Full democratization.

**The destination:** The Field of Reeds (Aaru) — a paradise that mirrors Egyptian life, perfected. Abundant fields, water, family, everything good about earthly existence without suffering.

**The major texts:**

### The Book of the Dead

Originally called *The Book of Coming Forth by Day*. Approximately 200 spells, most traceable to earlier Pyramid Texts and Coffin Texts. Each copy was unique — created specifically for the individual who would use it. The most famous copy is the Papyrus of Ani (c. 1250 BCE).

The Book of the Dead is the primary source for the dream quest's mechanics — the weighing of the heart, the 42 confessions, Ammit, the Field of Reeds.

### The Amduat

*That Which Is In the Afterworld.* Exclusively royal — inscribed on the walls of pharaohs' tombs in the Valley of the Kings. Describes Ra's nightly journey through the twelve hours of the underworld from sunset to dawn, with the pharaoh traveling alongside him.

**The 12 Hours:**

| Hour | Location | Key Event |
|------|----------|-----------|
| 1 | Western horizon (Akhet) | Ra enters the night. Sacred apes mourn his passage. |
| 2 | Wernes — abundant watery world | Ra passes through the Waters of Osiris |
| 3 | Waters of Osiris | The Teka-her serpent appears among the divine procession |
| 4 | Sandy realm of Sokar | Dark zig-zag pathways. Ra is dragged on a snake-boat. |
| 5 | Sokar's buried kingdom | The deepest realm. The barque cannot sail — it is dragged. Ra briefly glimpses the hidden form of Osiris. |
| 6 | Field of fire | Midnight. Greatest danger and greatest regeneration simultaneously. |
| 7 | Apep's ambush | Apep lies in wait. Subdued by the magic of Isis, the strength of Set, and Serqet. Most dangerous hour. |
| 8 | The tomb doors open | The dead awaken and receive light. |
| 9 | Return to water | Ra rows vigorously back into the waters, leaving Sokar's sandy island. |
| 10 | Immersion | Regeneration continues through water. |
| 11 | Eyes regenerated | Ra's eyes — symbols of his health and power — are fully restored. |
| 12 | Eastern horizon | Ra emerges reborn. The pharaoh, merged with Ra, is reborn with the dawn. |

**The main purpose of the Amduat:** To give the pharaoh the names of every god and monster in the underworld so he can call upon allies and defeat enemies by naming them. Knowledge of names is power.

**What this means for your campaign:** The Amduat is the direct source for Gate Two (Apep) and the general structure of the river of stars. The dead man's boat echoes Ra's solar barque. The party keeping the boat moving mirrors Ra's crew fighting to maintain forward progress through the night.

### The Book of Gates

Similar structure to the Amduat but organized around gates rather than hours. Gates regulate movement rather than measuring time — passage requires authorization, not just endurance.

12 gates, each guarded by a serpent deity associated with a goddess. The deceased must know the name of each guardian to pass. Failure means suffering in a lake of fire.

**Key difference from the Amduat:** Hours measure time. Gates measure worthiness. A soul may endure the night but still be denied passage. Knowledge alone is insufficient — the soul must be authorized.

The Book of Gates also notably states that all races of humanity — Egyptians, Nubians, Libyans, Asiatics — are welcome in the afterworld. This cosmopolitan spirit is unusual for the period.

**The fifth hour of the Book of Gates** depicts the judgment hall of Osiris — making it a direct precedent for the Hall of Two Truths in the dream quest.

**What this means for your campaign:** Gate Five (Isis, Nephthys, Selket) and the general gate structure draws from the Book of Gates tradition. The shift from Amduat hours to named gates mirrors the shift from endurance to authorization that defines the second half of the dream quest.

# THE SOUL'S COMPONENTS

The Egyptians understood the soul as multiple distinct components, not a single unified entity:

| Component | Name | Description |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| Ka | Life force | The vital essence. Requires a physical body to return to — hence mummification. |
| Ba | Personality | Depicted as a bird with a human head. Travels freely between the living world and the dead. |
| Akh | Transformed spirit | The union of Ka and Ba after successful passage. What the soul becomes in the afterlife. |
| Ib | Heart | The seat of morality. What is weighed on the scales. |
| Ren | Name | Identity itself. To speak someone's true name is to have power over them. |
| Sah | Spiritual body | Can interact with other spiritual beings in the afterlife. |
| Shut | Shadow | A component of the soul, less understood. |

**What this means for your campaign:** The Ren — the name — is the component that connects Egyptian soul theology directly to the Planar Binding mechanic. The wizard collects names. Names are power over souls. The dead man's true name appears in the sending stone reports alongside kings and gods. This is not accidental.

# THE COMPLETE AFTERLIFE JOURNEY
## New Kingdom Standard Version

This is the version the dream quest is based on.

### Before the Journey

**Mummification (70 days):**
- Body washed with Nile water
- Internal organs removed and placed in canopic jars under the protection of the Four Sons of Horus:
  - Imsety (human head) — liver
  - Duamutef (jackal head) — stomach
  - Hapy (baboon head) — lungs
  - Qebehsenuef (falcon head) — intestines
- Body dried with natron salt
- Stuffed with dry materials to maintain shape
- Wrapped in linen

**Opening of the Mouth Ceremony:**
Performed at the tomb before sealing. A priest touches the mummy's face with a ritual adze (the peseshkef), restoring the deceased's senses — sight, hearing, speech, taste. Without this the soul cannot function in the afterlife. Depicted in the Tomb 1 puzzle room paintings.

**Burial:**
Body placed in sarcophagus with grave goods, canopic jars, ushabti figurines (magical servants to perform labor in the afterlife), and funerary texts.

### The Journey

**Entry into the Duat:**
The soul enters the underworld. The Duat is vast, dangerous, and filled with hostile creatures, fire, serpents, and gates. Not a simple corridor — a complex realm combining elements of paradise, purgatory, and chaos.

**Guided by Anubis:**
Anubis guides the soul through the darkness, defends it from hostile creatures, escorts it to judgment. He is protector, guide, and judge's assistant simultaneously.

**Navigation:**
Spells from the Book of the Dead provide protection and instruction. Gate guardians must be named correctly to pass. The soul must know where it is going and who stands between it and its destination.

**Apep:**
The great serpent waits in the darkness of Hour 7. He attacks every night. He is never permanently destroyed — only driven back. His powers:
- Hypnotic gaze that paralyzes Ra and his defenders
- Ability to drink the celestial river dry, stranding the solar barque
- Control over darkness
- Association with natural disasters and solar eclipses (when he partially succeeded in swallowing the sun)

Defeated by: Set (the only god who could resist his hypnotic gaze), Ra in the form of a cat, magical arrows, binding spells, wax effigies mutilated and burned in ritual. The dead could participate in defeating Apep — some Book of the Dead spells are specifically for this purpose.

The *Books of Overthrowing Apep* describes a systematic process of dishonoring, dismembering, and disposing of Apep through ritual. Images of Apep were always shown with another deity subduing him — even his image was considered dangerous.

### Judgment

**The Hall of Two Truths:**
Also called the Hall of Ma'at. Presided over by Osiris on his throne, the blue veil of the afterlife behind him.

Present:
- **Osiris** — judge, seated on throne, crook and flail
- **Anubis** — operates the scales, escorts the heart
- **Thoth** — records the verdict on his palette
- **Isis and Nephthys** — flank Osiris's throne as protectors
- **Selket** — present with scorpion crown
- **The 42 Assessors of Ma'at** — seated in rows, each one associated with a specific sin
- **Ammit** — waits beside the scales

**The 42 Confessions (Declaration of Innocence):**
In standard Egyptian mythology these are *negative* confessions — "I have not stolen," "I have not lied." In your campaign they are *positive* confessions — "I have stolen," "I have lied." This change reflects your world's emphasis on truth over purity. The scales don't weigh whether you've sinned. They weigh whether you know yourself.

Each confession is addressed to one of the 42 divine judges by name. The soul must know the judges' names to address them correctly. Three judges appear in Tomb 3's inner chamber painting: Neha-hau, Am-khaibitu, Hept-Shet.

**The Weighing of the Heart (Psychostasia):**
Anubis places the heart (Ib) on one pan of the golden scales. The Feather of Ma'at sits on the other pan.

- **Heart lighter than or equal to the feather:** The soul is declared *maa-kheru* — "true of voice" — and admitted to paradise.
- **Heart heavier than the feather:** Ammit devours the heart. The soul ceases to exist entirely. This is the "second death" — total annihilation, not punishment or hell.

Thoth records the verdict. The result is announced to the assembled gods.

**Ammit:**
Part lion (front), part hippopotamus (rear), part crocodile (head). She does not move until she moves. She has never once been disappointed — whatever she waits for always arrives eventually. She is not evil in the Egyptian conception — she is a function of justice. She destroys what cannot be redeemed.

### After Judgment

**The Field of Reeds (Aaru):**
An idealized mirror of Egyptian life — abundant fields, water, family, the pleasures of earthly existence perfected and made permanent. The soul lives here eternally.

**The Blue Veil:**
Behind Osiris's throne. What lies beyond it is not described in Egyptian sources — it is the mystery beyond judgment, the territory of those who have passed. The living are not meant to see it.

# APEP — EXTENDED NOTES

Apep is the most important hostile entity in the dream quest. Full detail here for reference.

**Nature:** Not a god — a primordial force, a "non-being," the embodiment of chaos (Isfet) against which all order (Ma'at) must be maintained. No temples were built to him. He received no worship. He was feared, not revered.

**Origin:** Appears as early as the Pyramid Texts (Old Kingdom). Has existed as long as the concept of order has existed — he is its shadow.

**Powers:**
- Hypnotic gaze — paralyzes gods and mortals
- Can drink the celestial river dry
- Causes darkness, natural disasters, solar eclipses
- Eater of Souls — threatens the dead as well as the living
- Cannot be permanently destroyed — defeated every night, returns every sunset

**Defeating Apep:**
The *Books of Overthrowing Apep* describes the ritual in detail:
1. Make a wax model of Apep
2. Write his name on it in green ink
3. Spit on it
4. Mutilate it
5. Burn it
6. Recite the spells of overthrow

Egyptian priests performed this ritual daily to ensure Ra's safe passage. Images of Apep always included another deity subduing him — even his depiction was considered potentially dangerous.

**In the Amduat specifically:** Set fights Apep at Hour 7 with a spear. Isis uses magic. Serqet (scorpion goddess) assists. Ra's whole crew is involved. This is the most dangerous moment of the nightly journey.

**In your dream quest:** Apep is Gate Two — the largest, most primal danger on the river. The serpent from Gate One slips beneath the surface and reveals itself as Apep at Gate Two. The solution is keeping the boat moving, because Apep feeds on stillness and drift. He cannot catch what does not stop.

# CAMPAIGN CONNECTIONS

**The covenant of the road:**
The Amduat's stated purpose — giving the dead the names of every power in the underworld so that naming them commands them — is the seed of the campaign's whole names economy. In this world the funerary texts are the only place a demon's true name was ever written down: the covenant names the keepers of the dead's road gave the line-gods as sureties, handed down through the gods' own death-rites, in the age before binding existed. See [Demons, the Hotspot, and the Deep Cities](demons-and-the-hotspot.md).

**True names and the Ren:**
Egyptian soul theology treats the name (Ren) as a fundamental component of identity and existence. To know someone's true name is to have power over their soul. This maps directly onto the Planar Binding mechanic in your campaign — the wizard collects names, the demon wife was bound by her true name, and the party is harvesting names from the tombs without understanding what names mean in this theological context.

When the party eventually understands the wizard's operation, they will also understand — if they've been paying attention — that what he's doing has ancient precedent in a culture that understood names as the seat of power over souls.

**The Amseh dynasty and the failed afterlife:**
The four Amseh kings built their tombs according to Egyptian afterlife tradition. They performed the rituals. They inscribed the spells. They prepared. And the gods fell silent anyway. The climate collapsed. The children were sacrificed. The empire starved.

The empty thrones in Tomb 4 are the theological statement of the dynasty's end: we called to you. You did not come. We did everything right and it meant nothing.

Kethara remained in the valley. The gods did not.

**Ammit and Maalm:**
Ammit took up permanent residence in the ruins beneath what is now Maalm after a thousand years of condemned dead and the uneaten hearts of ten thousand innocent children. The founding of Maalm is directly connected to Egyptian afterlife mythology — Bedraageon fought the maddened devourer goddess and the city was built above them both.

The party walks on ground where Ammit sleeps. Or sleeps and something else, now. This has not yet been made explicit to them.

**The dream quest as rehearsal:**
The party shepherds one soul through a version of this journey. The campaign will eventually ask them to make decisions about binding, slavery, and the power of names on a much larger scale. Everything they experience in the dream quest — what it costs to be named, what it means to be bound, what Osiris weighs — is preparation for that decision.

They don't know that yet.
